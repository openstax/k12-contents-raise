Activity (20 minutes)

This activity introduces three different ways to multiply binomials together. Students should familiarize themselves with each method. Then they can decide which method works best for them and use the others to check their multiplication.

Launch

As a class, examine the three methods used in the table to multiply \((x-7)(2x+4)\). Go step by step through each method while asking for questions after each method. Then allow students to work through the exercises individually.

Anticipated Misconceptions

When students use the Distributive Property, emphasize that the sign combining the new products is determined by the sign in the binomial that is broken up. The example from the table is shown below, with the corresponding signs in red:

\((x\;{\style{color:red}-}\;7)(2x+4) = x(2x+4)\;{\style{color:red}-}\;7(2x+4)\)

Activity Synthesis

Ask students to explain which method they prefer. As students discuss each method, emphasize the following points:

  • To use the Distributive Property, the first binomial is broken up so that each term is multiplied by the second binomial.
  • When using Vertical Alignment, it is important to align like terms as you write the individual products. This will help you combine them into the correct final product.
  • When using the FOIL method, you can view the process as 4 individual steps that are then combined to find the product.

Just like there are different ways to represent multiplication of numbers, there are several different ways to represent how to multiply a binomial times a binomial using the Distributive Property of Multiplication over Addition.

Multiplying Binomials Using the FOIL Method

If you multiply binomials often enough, you may notice a pattern. Notice that the first term in the result is the product of the first terms in each binomial. The second and third terms are the product of multiplying the two outer terms and then the two inner terms. And the last term results from multiplying the two last terms.

We abbreviate “First, Outer, Inner, Last” as FOIL. The letters stand for “First, Outer, Inner, Last.” We use this as another way to multiply binomials. The word FOIL is easy to remember and ensures we find all four products.

Let’s multiply \((x+3)(x+7)\) using both methods.

We summarize the steps of the FOIL method below. The FOIL method only applies to multiplying binomials, not other polynomials!

How to use the FOIL method to multiply two binomials:

Step 1
Multiply the First terms.

Step 2
Multiply the Outer terms.

Step 3
Multiply the Inner terms.

Step 4
Multiply the Last terms.

Step 5
Combine like terms, when possible.


When you multiply by the FOIL method, drawing the arrows will help your brain focus on the pattern and make it easier to apply.

Now we will review examples where we use the FOIL pattern to multiply two binomials.

Multiply the binomials using the FOIL method.

  1. \((y-7)(y+4)\)

Step 1
Multiply the \({\style{color:red}F}{\style{color:red}i}{\style{color:red}r}{\style{color:red}s}{\style{color:red}t}\) terms.

Step 2
Multiply the \({\style{color:red}O}{\style{color:red}u}{\style{color:red}t}{\style{color:red}e}{\style{color:red}r}\) terms.

Step 3
Multiply the \({\style{color:red}I}{\style{color:red}n}{\style{color:red}n}{\style{color:red}e}{\style{color:red}r}\) terms.

Step 4
Multiply the \({\style{color:red}L}{\style{color:red}a}{\style{color:red}s}{\style{color:red}t}\) terms.

Step 5
Combine like terms.
\(y^2 - 3y - 28\)

  1. \((4x+3)(2x-5)\)

Step 1
Multiply the First terms, \( {\style{color:red}4} {\style{color:red}x} \; {\style{color:red}\cdot} \; {\style{color:red}2} {\style{color:red}x} \).

Step 2
Multiply the Outer terms, \( {\style{color:red}4} {\style{color:red}x}\; {\style{color:red}\cdot}\; {\style{color:red}(} {\style{color:red}{-5}} {\style{color:red})} \).

Step 3
Multiply the Inner terms, \( {\style{color:red}3}\; {\style{color:red}\cdot}\; {\style{color:red}2} {\style{color:red}x} \).

Step 4

\(8x^2-14x-15\)

The FOIL method is usually the quickest method for multiplying two binomials, but it only works for binomials. You can use the Distributive Property to find the product of any two polynomials.

Multiplying Binomials Using Vertical Alignment

Another representation that works for all polynomials is Vertical Alignment. It is much like the method you use to multiply whole numbers. Look carefully at this example of multiplying two-digit numbers.

\(\underline{\underline{\;\;\;\;23\\\times46}\\\;\;138\\\;\;92}\\1058\)

partial product
partial product
product

Start by multiplying 23 by 6 to get 138.
Next, multiply 23 by 4, lining up the partial product in the correct columns.
Finally, add the partial products.

  1. Multiply using Vertical Alignment: \((3y-1)(2y-6)\). It does not matter which binomial goes on the top.

Step 1
Multiply \(3y-1\) by \(-6\).

Step 2
Multiply \(3y-1\) by \(2y\).

Step 3
Add like terms.

Notice the partial products are the same as the terms in the FOIL method.
