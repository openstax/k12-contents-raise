    1. What is the value of \(5^0\)?

For positive whole numbers, \(5^a\cdot5^b=5^{a+b}\). For this law to still hold with 0 as an exponent, \(5^0\) needs to be equal to 1 because \(5^{0+4}=5^4\) and \(1\cdot5^4=5^4\).

Extending Your Thinking

We know, for example, that \((2+3)+5=2+(3+5)\) and \(2\cdot\left(3\cdot 5\right)=\left(2\cdot 3\right)\cdot 5\). The grouping with parentheses does not affect the value of the expression.

  1. Is this true for exponents? That is, are the numbers \(2^{\left(3^5\right)}\) and \(\left(2^3\right)^5\) equal? If not, which is bigger?

Enter your answer here:

Compare your answer:

The two numbers are not even close! Since \(3^5=243\), the number \(2^{\left(3^5\right)}\) is \(2^{243}\). On the other hand, \(\left(2^3\right)^5=2^3\cdot2^3\cdot2^3\cdot2^3\cdot2^3=2^{15}\). The number \(2^{243}\) is much larger than \(2^{15}\).



  1. Which of the two would you choose as the meaning of the expression \(2^{3^5}\) written without parentheses?

Enter your answer here:

Compare your answer:

Typically, we define repeated exponents by working down the tower, e.g., \(2^{3^5}=2^{\left(3^5\right)}\). One reason for this is that evaluating up the tower, as in \(\left(2^3\right)^5\), can already be simplified by using algebra rules (in this case, to get \(2^{15}\)).
