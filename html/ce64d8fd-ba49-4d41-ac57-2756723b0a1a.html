The National Council of Teachers of Mathematics (NCTM) encourages math teachers to not leave out the gifted students when they differentiate in the classroom. It is easy to assign more work or encourage students to help others who may be struggling. However, to fully benefit the gifted student, ensure that they are assigned open-ended, rich-inquiry activities instead of simply assigning more work or always using peer-tutoring and mentoring relationships. Differentiating is NOT adjusting the workload assigned based on ability levels or grading differently based on perceptions of students' capabilities (NCTM).

A gifted student:

  • Exhibits high-performance capability in an intellectual, creative, or artistic area;
  • Possesses an unusual capacity for leadership; or
  • Excels in a specific academic field. (Texas Education Code §29.121)

The OpenStax Algebra 1 curriculum enables students to extend the regular curriculum through differentiated instructional strategies such as:

  • New topics and ideas
  • Creative and critical thinking skills
  • Problem solving
  • Opportunities for inquiry
  • Independent study in student chosen topics
  • Advanced research methods. (Reis and Renzulli, 2018)

When you begin to use enrichment strategies in your Algebra classroom, ask yourself the following questions:

  1. Which enrichment strategies and activities meet the individual students in the class?
  2. Will the Algebra 1 content be differentiated through extension and enrichment?
  3. Will you replace the Algebra 1 foundational and introductory lessons with outsourced advanced content?
  4. How will you give students the opportunity to integrate personal interests into projects and assignments (Reis and Renzulli, 2018)?

In our section on differentiated learning, we shared how you can differentiate on content, product, or process. These same areas can be implemented in the Algebra I classroom to differentiate for gifted students.

  • Pacing: Provide opportunities for gifted learners to alter the typical pacing of a unit. Students could show what they know at the beginning of a unit, then move quicker through the already mastered materials.
  • Enrichment: Extend the curriculum through expanded reasoning, explorations, and investigations.
  • Sophistication: Integrate multiple content areas, discover new relationships, and examine new viewpoints.
  • Novelty: Bring in unique concepts, original curriculum, and creative expression of ideas for an individualized experience.
  • Project-Based Learning: Implement a culture of problem solving through project-based learning to help advanced students make connections to the real world; motivated by their own interests and experiences.
  • Research: Integrate multiple types of hands-on research to differentiate the learning process for your gifted students.
  • Advanced Products: Move students beyond writing papers to produce work that fits their individual strengths, interests, and learning profiles.
  • Oral Presentations: Provide opportunities for students to share their findings, products, and creative ideas.

One way that these differentiation strategies can be implemented within the Algebra 1 curriculum is through the projects offered within each unit. In the table below you will find examples of how you can differentiate the unit projects for gifted students including content, process, and product.

Unit Project Ideas Extending the Project

Unit 1 Project 1: Slopes and Intercepts

  1. Students analyze points on a graph to identify their meaning in context.
  2. Students identify slopes and intercepts from graphs and match equations to graphs.
  3. Students create their own scenarios to match a linear equation.

Product: Presentation

Ask gifted students to give a presentation on real-world scenarios that would require the use of slopes and intercepts.

Unit 2 Project 2: Modeling with Systems of Inequalities in Two Variables

Students engage in multiple aspects of mathematical modeling.

  1. Students interpret and analyze given models that represent the constraints and conditions in a situation.
  2. Students create their own models after specifying quantities of interest, identifying relevant information, and setting the constraints.

Content: Novelty

Ask students to work in pairs to come up with their own ingredients for the chart in Unit 2 Project, Activity P2. Then they can switch charts and research nutritional information.

Unit 3 Project 3: Two-Variable Statistics

Students collect, summarize, interpret, and draw conclusions from bivariate data using scatter plots, best fit lines, residuals, and correlation coefficients.

Product: Technology Integration

Technology isn’t required for this lesson, but there are opportunities for students to choose to use appropriate technology to solve problems.

Unit 4 Project 4: Using Functions to Model Battery Power

Students use functions to model real-life situations.

Both activities in the lesson use the context of a cell phone’s battery power varying over time. The relationships in each activity can be appropriately modeled with linear functions, though some students may choose to use a piecewise function in the second activity.

  1. Students are given a table of values showing times and percentages of battery power. They study the rates of change in the table, which are fairly constant, and produce a simple model to make a prediction.
  2. Students analyze images of battery-usage status from a cell phone. To gather the necessary information, students need to make sense of the numerical values and the graph on each image. They also need to determine how to organize and represent the information before they can use it to create a model.

Process: Research

The modeling demand can be adjusted further—for example, by asking students to collect data from their own electronic devices, or to create presentations to explain their models.

Product: Technology Integration

Technology isn’t required for this lesson, but there are opportunities for students to choose to use appropriate technology to solve problems.

Unit 5 Project 5: Introduction to Exponential Functions

In this lesson, students use the main function types they have studied thus far in the course (linear and exponential) to model different populations.

  1. Data for three city populations are given and students are asked to produce a linear or exponential model for each (if appropriate) and then make predictions for populations at other dates. The cities have been chosen so that one is well modeled by an exponential model, another by a linear model, and the third by neither.
  2. Students examine world population. The task is more open-ended and only limited data is provided, likely requiring students to gather more data. In addition, the world population is not consistently well modeled by a linear or exponential function. However, for certain periods of time, exponential and/or linear functions can be appropriate. In particular, in recent years the growth has been strikingly linear.

Content: Sophistication

Students engage in different parts of the modeling cycle. This can be adjusted further, for example, by choosing other cities for the first activity and having students find the data.

Students can think of a problem where population modeling would be critical to finding a solution. They can decide if solutions better support linear or exponential models.

Unit 6 Project 6: Polynomials and Rectangles

Students use polynomials to model dimensions and areas of rectangles. Both activities in the lesson use the dimensions and areas of rectangles to apply the skills of working with polynomials learned throughout the unit.

  1. Students are given diagrams of rectangles with side lengths labeled. They complete a table to find the missing measures of length, width, or area.
  2. Students are given a table of measures of rectangles, similar to the table in the previous activity. In this activity, they will not have the visual of the rectangles. Also, they will be given the area of rectangles and asked to find the dimensions. This will require division of polynomials or factoring.
  3. Students create their own table with given measures. Each student trades tables with a partner and works to find the missing measures. Students check their answers with their partner.

Product: Presentation

A discussion after the activity can include having students share the methods they used to create the table.

Unit 7 Project 7: Design a Fountain

In this culminating project, students use the fact that the shape made by water coming out of a jet or fountain can be modeled with a quadratic equation. Students will design a fountain with given criteria.

  1. Students work through a simplified example with one fountain and one jet. Students will think about what information is needed to write the equation. They can use a sketch to draw the path. Students decide on some reasonable measurements and write the equation. Understanding the use of the vertex form of a quadratic equation is helpful.
  2. Students are given an opportunity to design a fountain with three to five jets. Students are given certain measurements and criteria to meet to write the equation for paths of the jets.

Product: Presentation

Students may present their equations and an explanation of what information they used and how they wrote the equation of the path of the jets.

Unit 8 Project 8: Modeling Rocket Flight

Students use their knowledge of quadratic equations in different forms to solve problems.

  1. Students are given a graph of a quadratic function and are asked to write the function in factored form. Students will determine how many solutions a function has based on an understanding of its factored form. They will also be able to explain the characteristics of the graph of a quadratic function with no solution.
  2. Students work in pairs to fluently rewrite quadratic expressions in factored and standard form using the strategies learned in the unit.
  3. Students find the specific equation in standard form of a quadratic function that has certain zeros and passes through a particular point, given a real-world situation. They will use the equation to make a prediction about the situation, given the context of the problem.

Process: Model of Inquiry

Guide students to research Kathryn Johnson who is featured in the biopic Hidden Figures which tells the story of the impact of the mathematicians behind early space flight. Ask open ended questions like, “How did quadratic equations impact NASA early flights?”

Unit 9 Project 9: Using Quadratic Equations to Model Situations and Solve Problems

Students synthesize methods of solving quadratic equations and graphing quadratic functions to answer questions about quadratic functions within a context. They use tools to grapple with solving problems, without scaffolding, about a quadratic function that represents a context, and finding the points of intersection of a parabola and a line.

The lesson consists of two substantial problems. You might differentiate the learning by allowing students to choose one of the two problems.

Content: Pacing

Product: Presentation

You might decide to have all students attempt both problems and select students with different approaches to share their solutions with the class.

Product: Advanced Products

Students can prepare a visual display of their solution, conducting a gallery walk or a group presentation at the end of the lesson.

Product: Technology Integration

Technology isn't required for this lesson, but there are opportunities for students to choose to use appropriate technology to solve problems.
