Inquiry Project Unit 6 Overview

Lesson Narrative

In this project, students will be introduced to an area model as a tool for finding products. They will start with a numerical area model, focusing on two-digit numbers and discovering that you can partition a rectangle into smaller areas whose sum is the total area of the rectangle. They will then apply this area model to algebraic expressions, noticing that an area model can be used to multiply polynomials.

Students will first be asked to play with a simulation for a few minutes. This open play time allows students to learn how to use a simulation for learning, discover how they can interact with it, and feel ownership of their learning from the simulation. Following open play, students can share how to use the simulation, so every student is aware of the controls and how to interact.

When students are engaging with the activity sheet, they should be pausing to discuss some of their responses with a partner. The whole class discussions should produce a rich variety of thoughts and observations based on individual responses and partner discussions.

By the end of the lesson, students should have completed the activity sheets using the simulation, and should be able to use an area model to multiply two two-digit numbers or two polynomials, and explain why an area model can be used to find the product of two factors.

Learning Goals (Teacher Facing)

Students will be able to:

  • Describe the product of two factors as the area of a rectangle and a sum of partial products

  • Justify the product of two multi-digit numbers using an area model

  • Justify the product of two polynomials using an area model

    • Learning Targets (Student Facing)

  • I can multiply two two-digit numbers using an area model.

  • I can multiply two polynomials using an area model.

    • Activity Synthesis

    Using an area model with numbers before variables allows students to connect their understanding of the area of a rectangle to algebraic thinking, namely the distributive property. When we multiply two numbers, we are essentially finding the area of a rectangle with a length and a width that are equivalent to those two factors. Since we will be talking about multiplying algebraic expressions, we could apply the same thinking as we do with the area of a rectangle. Before we can do that, students need to have a solid understanding of the area of a rectangle as the product of two factors. Starting with whole numbers is a jumping off point for students to see the distributive property in action with partial products that make sense.

    If students are struggling at the start of this lesson, you could consider asking them to multiply 15✕18 by hand, and see what that process is like. Save their work and compare it with their area model. Which one makes more sense to them?

    Activity IP6.3: USING AN AREA MODEL

    Project Activity (20 minutes)

    In the activity, students will use an area model to find the product of two two-digit numbers. We want students to notice the total area of their models is consistent, and notice that partitioning by place value is "easier" to add up the parts than partitioning somewhere random. Another goal here is to have students see that they can group like-terms (in this case, all of the integers), to support them later with algebra tiles. Finally, students can test their knowledge of factors, partial products, and total area by playing an area model game.

    Launch

  • Allow students to work independently to answer questions on their activity sheet, stopping where indicated to have small-group or whole-class discussions.

  • Circulate as students work on Part 3 of the activity sheet, noting interesting responses. After you notice that groups have discussed #1c, pause and bring the class together for a whole-class discussion.

  • Call on groups to share out what is the same and what is different in their various area models.

  • Ask if any partitions were noticeably "easier" to add than others.

  • Encourage students to test their area models using the Generic screen in the simulation.

  • A common error might be partitioning 17 into 1 and 7 instead of 10 and 7. If you notice students making this error, ask them what the total side length of their rectangle is to push them to see their error.

    Activity IP6.4: APPLYING AN AREA MODEL

    In the activity, students will be introduced to an area model with variables. This may be a challenging step, but remind students that the side lengths in an area model have been sums up to now (ex: \(10+7\)) and an expression is just another sum. Once students have finished this activity, they should be able to make comparisons between area models with numbers and area models with variables.

    Answer: \(x2 - 2x - 15\). The total area of this rectangle is \((x)(x) + (x)(3) + (-5)(x) + (-5)(3) = x2 + 3x - 5x - 15 = x2 - 2x - 15\).

    6. How is multiplying variable expressions using an area model similar to multiplying numbers using an area model?
