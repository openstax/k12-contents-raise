Inquiry Project Unit 4 Overview

Lesson Narrative

In this project, students will apply their prior knowledge about plotting coordinate points, evaluating expressions using the correct order of operations, and simplifying algebraic expressions, to generate initial ideas about functions.

Students will first be asked to play with a simulation for a few minutes. This open play time allows students to learn how to use a simulation for learning, discover how they can interact with it, and feel ownership of their learning from the simulation. Following open play, students can share how to use the simulation, so every student is aware of the controls and how to interact.

When students are engaging with the activity sheet, they should be pausing to discuss some of their responses with a partner. The whole class discussions should produce a rich variety of thoughts and observations based on individual responses and partner discussions.

By the end of the lesson, students should have completed the activity sheets using the simulation, and should be able to describe a function as having inputs, outputs, and behaving consistently. They should also be able to evaluate a function using different inputs, and be able to summarize the impact that certain operations have on the algebraic function, the table of values, the appearance of the graph, and the outputs.

Learning Goals (Teacher Facing)

Students will be able to:

  • Define a function as a rule relating each input to exactly one output and consistently acting on inputs.

  • Predict outputs of a function using given inputs.

  • Describe a linear function in terms of a table, graph, equation, or series of operations.

    • Learning Targets (Student Facing)

  • I can define a function.

  • I can predict the output of a function using a given input.

  • I can describe a linear function in terms of a table, graph, equation, or series of operations.

  • ALG1.1(B) Use a problem-solving model that incorporates analyzing given information, formulating a play or strategy, determining a solution, justifying the solution, and evaluating the problem-solving process and the reasonableness of the solution.

  • ALG1.1(E) Create and use representations to organize, record, and communicate mathematical ideas.

  • ALG1.12(B) Decide whether relations represented verbally, tabularly, graphically, and symbolically define a function.

  • ALG1.12(C) Evaluate functions, expressed in function notation, given one or more elements in their domains.

    • Activity IP4.1: Simulating Relationships

    Warm Up (5 - 10 minutes)

    This warm up activity seeks to activate students’ intuition about the relationship between input and output by allowing them to investigate this relationship with images and numbers.
    The activity also provides students with key experiences that will anchor more abstract concepts such as domain, range, function notation, etc.

    Launch

    Instruct students to play with the Function Builder for 5 minutes.

    Encourage them to share what they are trying or investigating with their group members. If needed, provide students with the question prompts: “What did you try? What happened? How did you create that?” And the sentence stem, “I tried … and it caused … to happen.” Note that students may investigate any of the screens: Patterns, Numbers, or Equations.

    Circulate, addressing logistical concerns with accessing the simulation and observing student interactions and discoveries.

    Student-Facing Statement

    Today you are going to investigate something called an interactive simulation.

    • To access the simulation, go here and start playing with the simulation.

    • Once the activity is loaded, play with the simulation.

    • As you discover how the tool works, share your thinking with your group members. You may want to use the following question and sentence stem to guide your discussions:
      • What did you try? What happened?
      • I tried … and it caused … to happen.

    • Write down three things you notice or have questions about on your activity sheet.

    • Be prepared to share your findings with the whole class.

    Student Response

    Answers will vary. Sample student responses may include:

  • When I put something into the tube, it comes out the other end.

  • Nothing happened until I pulled one of the bottom arrows into the tube.

  • The camera made the pictures become black and white.

  • The rainbow changes the color of the pictures.

  • Sometimes I can drag a card backwards, sometimes I can't.

    • Activity Synthesis

    After 5 minutes, bring the class together for a brief discussion about what they noticed.
    Project the sim so that you can have a student come to the front to demonstrate their thinking. Solicit student responses to the following questions:

    1. What is something you noticed you can do in this simulation?

    2. What happens when you drag a card through the builder?

    3. What does the erase button do?

    4. What does this checkbox do?

    5. What questions do you have? (Write down student questions that you want to have answered by the end of the activity.)

    6. Which cards seemed interesting to you? (May like functions like the Warhol soup can, mystery C, or inputs like the planet. Be sure someone mentions the “x” input on the Equations screen- if they don't, show them.)

    Students will start to make meaning of a function through noticing patterns, and noticing that every screen in this simulation has cards on the left, right, and cards on the bottom. It is most important at this stage of the project that students understand how to use the simulation and trust that they have ownership over how they interact with it.

  • Ensure students notice that they have a choice to compose one function or up to three.

  • Also, if students have not observed that outputs update when new functions have been added to the “machine,” bring it to their attention.

  • If students use multiple functions, press them to consider if the order of the functions makes a difference as to the output.

    • Activity IP4.2: What is a function?

    Project Activity (15 minutes)

    This activity will get students interacting directly with a function so they can discover the basic principles of a function. It is important that students can name the parts of a function, come up with an initial definition of a function, be able to describe a function, and see that a function always behaves consistently.

    Launch

  • Allow students to work independently to answer questions on their activity sheet, stopping where indicated to have small-group or whole-class discussions.

  • Circulate as students work on Part 2 of the activity sheet, noting interesting responses. After you notice that groups have discussed #2, pause and bring the class together for a whole-class discussion.

  • Call on groups to share out some of their names. Write them on the board. If there are a lot of responses, circle a few that you will agree mean input, output, and function or operation.Allow students the freedom to use the names they prefer.

  • After you notice that individuals have finished #3, pause and bring the class together for a whole-class discussion.

  • Call on students to share out their definitions in #3. Lead class in combining the various answers to write a definition as a class.

  • When you notice groups discussing #4, give a 2 minutes time warning.

    1. Play with Function Builder for 5 minutes. Write down two things you notice or have questions about, and one thing a neighbor noticed that is interesting to you.

    2. Come up with your own names for these parts of a function:

    3. Share your labels with your group. Describe the similarities below. 💬

    4. The blue “builder” in the middle of the screen is a function. Describe what a function is using the labels you've agreed on as a class. 💬

    5. Suppose you build this function:

      1. Complete the following table, sketching what happens after going through the function.

        2. Input Output

      Student Response

      Answers will vary.

      1. Students are asked to generate names for the input images, the output images, and the function itself. Examples might include:
        • Front loader, inserts, domain, original, start
        • processor, machine, coder, producer, action
        • product, yield, after, end

      2. Circulate around the room to hear similarities between groups. Write student ideas on the board to give value to contributions. Add “input,” “output,” “function,” to the lists and share with students that these are terms that we’ll see in future lessons to represent these concepts, and their answers aren’t wrong, but we need to use the same language everywhere so we all know what we’re talking about!

      3. A sample student response: The function turns the input into the output. It is how the value is changed.

        1. Input Output

        2. A sample student responses may include: The function flips the image upside down (but not as a mirror does). The function rotates the image 180 degrees. The function turns the image around.

      Activity Synthesis

      The use of multiple inputs allows students to see that the function behaves consistently, and the simulation mimics building an input/output table. The use of the “see-inside” boxes equate to the use of process columns when building tables.

      Of note is the ability for students to investigate if functions are invertible. This provides an opportunity to discuss why some outputs can be pulled back through some functions but not others. If students discover this, allow them to share with the class, even though it is beyond the scope of today’s lesson.

      Allowing students to investigate the concept of a function with visual images provides them with mathematical experience that sets them up to investigate functions using numbers.

    Activity IP4.3: Different Kinds of Functions

    Project Activity (20 minutes)

    In the Numbers Screen activity, students will explore an arithmetic function using the terms “input” and “output.” As students build a function, the equation representation updates dynamically and includes a simplified representation. Students can observe the resulting outputs as in the previous activity, and as an explicit table of ordered pairs.

    In the Equations Screen activity, students build on their experiences by shifting from “input” and “output” to “x” and “y” and adding the representation of a graph for the function values. This permits students to wonder and explore how changing different parameters in the function will impact the outputs, the table of values, and the graph.

    Launch

  • Circulate as students work on part 3. If some groups seem behind, offer assistance if they have questions.

  • After 5 minutes or so, give students a time update that they should be on to #4.

  • When groups have finished, bring the class together for a whole-class discussion. Have four different students share their responses to #6.

    4. Build a custom function. Fill in the function builder and representations.

    5. What is y when x is 100?

    6. Manipulate your function in different ways. Describe the effects on the table, graph, and equation that each of your actions has.

    Student Response

    Answers will vary. Sample student responses are provided.

    1. Sample equation: \(Output = Input2 + 2\)

    2. Response using sample equation listed above: \(7 = 102 + 2\)

    3. Response using sample equation listed above: Divide the input by two and then add two. Two more than half the input. Etc. Sample equation: \(y = -1(x - 3)+ 1 or y = -x + 4\)

    4. Response using sample equation listed in question 4: \(y = -100 + 4 = -96\)

    5. Responses based on sample equation may include:

      6. Action Effect on table Effect on graph Effect on equation
      Click the up arrow on the addition operation When I clicked up, the \(y\) values increased by 1 for each click. The graph moved up the number of spaces that I had clicked. The number at the end of the equation changed (constant term).
      Click the down arrow on the addition operation If I clicked down, it decreased 1 unit for each click. The graph moved down the number of spaces that I had clicked. The number at the end of the equation changed (constant term).
      Click on the up or down arrows on the multiplication operation The numbers in the table got farther apart when I went up or down. At one point they were 2 units apart then after I clicked it, they were 3 units apart. Something weird happened when the multiplier was 0. At zero, everything just equaled 1 because the zero got rid of everything else. This changed the slope or tilt of the line. When I clicked up, the direction changed to where the line tilted up as I moved to the right. When I clicked down, the line got steeper when it slanted down as I moved to the right. Both numbers in the equation changed - the one at the end (constant term) and the one in front of the x (coefficient).
      Click on the up or down arrows on the subtraction operation When I click down, all the y-values move down 1 unit, too. The graph slid down the y-axis by 1 unit each click. The slope or tilt of the line did not change. In the equation, the value inside the parentheses changed with each click. In the simplified equation, this changed the last number (constant term).

    6. I can make a line. I can only make lines with this function.

    7. In general, students might have provided responses similar to:
      a. The addition operation impacted the graph by moving it up or down. Adding more makes the graph move up.
      b. The subtraction operation impacted the graph by moving it up or down. Subtracting more makes the graph move down.
      c. The multiplication operation impacted the graph by changing how steep the line was and whether it increased or decreased (moving from left to right on the graph). Multiplying more makes the graph steeper up and down.
      d. The division operation impacted the graph by changing how steep the line was and whether it increased or decreased (moving to the right on the graph). Dividing more makes the graph flatter.

    Activity Synthesis

    Ask students to answer the following question on an exit ticket. Review after class to determine student misunderstandings and address in a subsequent day's warm up.
    1. What is a function?
    2. What are the different ways a function can be represented?

    Sample student responses:

    A function describes the relationship between an input and an output. Every input has a single output, no matter how many times you put it through the function. Different inputs could have the same output though. A function is a rule that takes an input, does something to it, and makes an output.

    A function can be represented with words, table of values, list of points, a graph, or an equation.

    Optional extension: Play with the game! Challenge yourself to figure out the mystery functions. They get tricky when you have two or three operations. Does your rule match the simulation's rule? Can there be more than one answer?
