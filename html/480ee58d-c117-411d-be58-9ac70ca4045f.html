In this culminating project, students use their knowledge of quadratic equations in different forms to solve problems.

In the first activity, students are given a graph of a quadratic function and are asked to write the function in factored form. Students will determine how many solutions a function has based on an understanding of its factored form. They will also be able to explain the characteristics of the graph of a quadratic function with no solution.

In the second activity, students work in pairs to fluently rewrite quadratic expressions in factored and standard form using the strategies learned in the unit.

In the third activity, students find the specific equation in standard form of a quadratic function that has certain zeros and passes through a particular point, given a real-world situation. They will use the equation to make a prediction about the situation, given the context of the problem.


Project Activities

  1. Relating the Graphs of Quadratic Equations to Their Factored Form by Their Zeros (5 minutes)
  2. Converting Between Factored Form and Standard Form (20 minutes)
  3. Modeling Rocket Flight Using a Quadratic Equation (15 minutes)

Learning Objectives
Learning Goals (Teacher Facing) Learning Targets (Student Facing)
  • Explain why quadratic equations can have no solutions and explain why there are none.
  • Recognize that the number of solutions to a quadratic equation can be revealed when the equation is written as an expression in factored form equal to zero.
  • Write an equivalent expression in factored form given a quadratic expression of the form \(ax^2+ bx + c\), where \(a\) is not 1.
  • Use the intercept form of a quadratic function, \(f(x) = a(x − p)(x − q)\), to define a specific function given its zeros and a point.
  • Explain why quadratic equations can have no solutions and explain why there are none.
  • Recognize quadratic equations that have zero, one, or two solutions when they are written in factored form.
  • Write equivalent expressions in factored form when given quadratic expressions of the form \(ax^2+ bx + c\) and \(a\) is not 1.
  • Use the intercept form of a quadratic function to define a specific function given its zeros and a point.
