Activity (20 minutes)

In this activity, students are given polynomials without any additional context. They must choose a strategy to factor them appropriately and defend their choice with reasoning.

Launch

Take 2–3 minutes to review the diagram at the start of the activity as a class. Using the sample polynomial from the table, \(4x^2 + 20xy + 25y^2\), find its correct place in the diagram and corresponding strategy. (perfect square trinomial of the form \(a^2 + 2ab + b^2\))

Allow students 10–12 minutes to complete questions 1–7 individually. Leave 3–5 minutes for students’ questions regarding appropriate strategies.

For an additional challenge, pose the polynomial in Extending Your Thinking. The factored solution requires multiple strategies implemented in the correct order.

Extending Your Thinking

Factor the polynomial below. Write down every factoring strategy that you use.

\(9x^2 − 12xy + 4y^2 − 49\)

ANSWER: \((3x − 2y −7)(3x − 2y + 7)\); Strategies Used: 

Factor out GCF, grouping, perfect square trinomial, difference of squares

Here is how tto factor this polynomial:

Step 1
Is there a GCF?
\(9x^2−12xy+4y^2−49\)
No.

Step 2
With more than three terms, use grouping. The last two terms have no GCF. Try grouping the first three terms.

Step 3
Factor the trinomial with \(a \neq 1\). But the first term is a perfect square.
Is the last term of the trinomial a perfect square?
\((3x)^2−12xy+(2y)^2−49\)
Yes.

Step 4
Does the trinomial fit the pattern \(a^2 \pm 2ab + b^2\)?
( 3 x ) 2 ↘ −12 x y + −2 ( 3 x ) ( 2 y ) ↙ ( 2 y ) 2 − 49 ( 3 x ) 2 ↘ −12 x y + −2 ( 3 x ) ( 2 y ) ↙ ( 2 y ) 2 − 49
Yes.

Step 5
Write the trinomial as a square.
\((3x−2y)^2−49\)

Step 6
Is this binomial a sum or difference? Of squares or cubes? Write it as a difference of squares.
\((3x−2y)^2−7^2\)

Step 7
Write it as a product of conjugates.
\(((3x−2y)−7)((3x−2y)+7)\)
\((3x−2y −7)(3x−2y +7)\)

Step 8
Is the expression factored completely?
Yes.

Check your answer by multiplying.
\((3x−2y−7)(3x−2y+7)\)

Activity Synthesis

Pose the following questions to students:

  • Which strategy is applicable to factoring binomials? (difference of squares; factoring the GCF)
  • Which strategy is applicable to factoring trinomials of the form \(x^2 + bx +c\)? (undoing FOIL)
  • Which strategy is applicable to factoring trinomials of the form \(ax^2 + bx +c\)? (trial and error, the “\(ac\)” method)
  • Which strategy is applicable to factoring trinomials of the form \(ax^2 \pm 2ab + b^2\)? (perfect square trinomials)
  • Which strategy is applicable to factoring polynomials with \(4\) expressions? (grouping)
  • Which strategy is applicable to polynomials that don’t seem to factor easily? (substitution, grouping, factoring the GCF)

Reviewing General Strategies to Factor a Polynomial

You have now become acquainted with all the methods of factoring that you will need in this course. The following chart summarizes all the factoring methods we have covered and outlines a strategy you should use when factoring polynomials.

These are the steps to use when deciding how to factor a polynomial. We will review an example after looking at these steps.


Use a general strategy for factoring polynomials:

Step 1
Is there a greatest common factor? Factor it out.

Step 2
Is the polynomial a binomial, a trinomial, or are there more than three terms?

If it is a binomial:

  • Is it a sum?
    • Of squares? Sums of squares do not factor.
    • Of cubes? Use the sum of cubes pattern.
  • Is it a difference?
    • Of squares? Factor as the product of conjugates.

If it is a trinomial:

  • Is it of the form \(x^2+bx+c\)? Undo FOIL.
  • Is it of the form \(ax^2+bx+c\)?
    • If \(a\) and \(c\) are squares, check if it fits the trinomial square pattern.
    • Use trial and error or the “\(ac\)” method.

If it has more than three terms:

  • Use the grouping method.

Step 3
Check.

  • Is it factored completely?
  • Do the factors multiply back to the original polynomial?

EXAMPLE 1

Factor completely: \(24y^2 − 150\).

Be careful when you are asked to factor a binomial because there are several options!

Step 1
Is there a GCF?
\(24y^2 − 150\)
Yes, \(6\).

Step 2
Factor out the GCF.
\(6(4y^2 − 25)\)

Step 3
In the parentheses, is it a binomial, a trinomial, or are there more than three terms?
Binomial.

Step 4
Is it a sum?
No.

Step 5
Is it a difference? Of squares or cubes?
Yes, squares.
\(6((2y)^2 − (5)^2)\)

Step 6
Write as a product of conjugates.
\( 6(2y − 5)(2y + 5)\)

Step 7
Is the expression factored completely? 
Neither binomial can be factored.

Step 8
Check your answer by multiplying.
\(6(2y − 5)(2y + 5)\)
\(6(4y^2 − 25)\)
\(24y^2 − 150✓\)

Solution

Often, there will be more than one strategy that can be used to factor a polynomial. Here are some sample solutions.

  1. Factor out the GCF of \(4y\). Use the undo FOIL method on the remaining polynomial since it is of the form \(x^2+bx+c\).
  1. Factor out GCF of \(4\). Then factor by grouping since there are four terms.
