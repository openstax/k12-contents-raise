For integer inputs, we can think of multiplication as repeated addition and exponentiation as repeated multiplication:

\(3 \cdot 5=3+3+3+3+3\) and \(3^5=3 \cdot 3 \cdot 3 \cdot 3 \cdot 3\).

We could continue this process with a new operation called tetration. It uses the symbol \(\uparrow \uparrow\), and it is defined as repeated exponentiation:

\(3 \uparrow \uparrow 5=3^{3 \wedge 3 \wedge 3 \wedge 3}\).

Compute \(2 \uparrow \uparrow 3\) and \(3 \uparrow \uparrow 2\). If \(f(x)=3 \uparrow \uparrow x\), what is the relationship between \(f(x)\) and \(f(x+1)\)?

Compare your answer:

We have \(2 \uparrow \uparrow 3=2^{2 \wedge 2}=24=16\) and \(3 \uparrow \uparrow 2=3^3=27\).

The numbers output by this function get large absurdly fast, even in comparison to exponential functions. We calculated \(f(2)=27\) above, and now \(f(3)=3^{f(2)}=3^{27}=7,625,597,484,987\).
