Warm Up (5 minutes)

The purpose of this warm up is to help students recall finding the slope of a line. Remind students that slope is the ratio of the change in \( y \) and the change in \( x \). Substituting is an important skill in writing equations.

Launch

Arrange students in groups of 2. Encourage students to write out each step and to use parentheses, especially when working with negative numbers.

Student Activity

Use the slope formula, \( m=\frac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1} \), to find the slope of the line through the given points.

  1. \( (1, -2) \) and \( (3, 4) \)

Answer:

1. \( m= 3 \)

First, name \( (1, −2) \) as point 1 and \( (3, 4) \) as point 2.
\( (x_1, y_1) \) and \( (x_2, y_2) \)
\( (1,−2) \) and \( (3, 4) \)

Use the slope formula.

\( m=\frac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1} \)
Substitute the values: \( y_2 = 4, y_1 = -2, x_2 = 3, x_1 = 1 \).
\( m=\frac{4-(-2)}{3-(1)} \)

Simplify.

\( m=\frac{6}{2} \)
\( m= 3 \)

  1. \( (-2, -3) \) and \( (-7, 4) \)

Answer:

\( m= -\frac{7}{5} \)
First, name \( ( −2, −3) \) as point 1 and \( (−7, 4) \) as point 2.
\( (x_1, y_1) \) and \( (x_2, y_2) \)
\( ( −2,−3)\) and \( (−7, 4) \)

Use the slope formula.

\( m=\frac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1} \)
Substitute the values: \( y_2 = 4, y_1 = −3, x_2 = −7, x_1 = −2 \).
\( m= \frac{4-(-3)}{-7-(-2)} \)

Simplify.

\( m= \frac{7}{-5} \)
\( m= -\frac{7}{5} \)

Student Response

Anticipated Misconceptions

Some students may be confused by the subscripts. Point out that the notation helps make sure that you are matching the correct \( x \)-value with the corresponding \( y \)-value. Demonstrate that you can name either point as \( (x_1, y_1) \) by having students find the slope by naming \( (-7, 4) \) as \( (x_1, y_1) \) and \( (-2,-3) \) as \( (x_2, y_2) \).

Activity Synthesis

Ask students to share their methods for finding the slope.

Discuss with students:

  • “How do you decide which point to use for \( (x_1, y_1) \) and \( (x_2, y_2) \)?” (Your answer may vary, but here is a sample. I substituted the first coordinate pair as \((x_1,y_1)\).)
  • “Does it matter which point you choose for \( (x_1, y_1) \) and \( (x_2, y_2) \)?” (No.)
  • “How can you make sure you use the correct corresponding \( x \) for each \( y \)?” (Your answer may vary, but here is a sample: Always start with the \(y\) in the second point, so start with \(x\) in the second point).
