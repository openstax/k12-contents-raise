Warm Up (10 minutes)

This warm up activity prompts students to recall strategies for multiplying mentally.

Each expression can be evaluated in different ways. For example, \(19 \cdot 21\) can be viewed as \(19 \cdot (20 + 1)\), as \(21 \cdot (20 − 1)\), or as \((20 − 1) \cdot (20 + 1)\), among other ways. Reasoning flexibly about the structure of numerical expressions encourages students to do the same when rewriting quadratic expressions in this lesson and beyond.

Launch

Display one problem at a time. Give students quiet time to think individually for each problem and ask them to give a signal when they have an answer and a strategy. Keep all problems displayed throughout the talk. Follow with a whole-class discussion.

Support for English Language Learners and Students with Disabilities

Speaking: MLR8 Discussion Supports. Display sentence frames to support students when they explain their strategy. For example, “First, I _____ because . . .” or “I noticed _____ so I . . . .” Some students may benefit from the opportunity to rehearse what they will say with a partner before they share with the whole class.

Design Principle(s): Optimize output (for explanation)

Representation: Internalize Comprehension. To support working memory, provide students with sticky notes or mini whiteboards. Supports accessibility for: Memory; Organization

Student Activity

Finding a product mentally can be done by changing the way you look at a multiplication problem.

For example, \(9 \cdot 39\) can be thought of as \(9 \cdot (40 − 1)\). For some people, this might be easier to calculate mentally since you can imagine the solution as \(360 − 9\), or 351. This method leverages the distributive property of multiplication over addition.

For the following questions, find each product mentally. Then, explain how you changed the way you looked at each multiplication problem to use the distributive property.

  1. (\(9 \cdot 11\))

Answer: 99
For example, \(9 \cdot 11\) can be thought of as \(9 \cdot (10 + 1)\). This simplifies to \(90 + 9\), or 99.

  1. (\(19 \cdot 21\))

Answer: 399
For example, \(19 \cdot 21\) can be thought of as \(19 \cdot (20 + 1)\). This simplifies to \(380 + 19\), or 399.

  1. (\(99 \cdot 101\))

Answer: 9999
For example, \(99 \cdot 101\) can be thought of as \(99 \cdot (100 + 1)\). This simplifies to \(9900 + 99\), or 9999.

  1. (\(109 \cdot 101\))

Answer: 11,009
For example, \(109 \cdot 101\) can be thought of as \(109 \cdot (100 + 1)\). This simplifies to \(10,900 + 109\), or 11,009.

Activity Synthesis

Ask students to share their strategies for each problem. Record and display their responses for all to see. To involve more students in the conversation, consider asking:

  • "Who can restate _______’s reasoning in a different way?"
  • "Did anyone have the same strategy but would explain it differently?"
  • "Did anyone solve the problem in a different way?"
  • "Does anyone want to add on to _______’s strategy?"
  • "Do you agree or disagree? Why?"
