Project Activity (10 minutes)

Launch

The purpose of this activity is for students to use what they know to solve an unfamiliar problem. This is a preview of solving a system consisting of a linear equation and a quadratic equation algebraically and graphically, which students will study more in depth in a future course.

Student Activity

Here are graphs of a linear function and a quadratic function. The quadratic function is defined by the expression \((x−4)^2−5\).

Find the coordinates of \(P\), \(Q\), and \(R\) without using graphing technology. Show your reasoning.

Student Response

\(P=(0,11)\)

  • The quadratic expression \((x−4)^2−5\) can be rewritten in standard form as \(x^2−8x+11\). The constant term 11 means the \(y\)-intercept is \((0,11)\).

\(Q=(3,-4)\) and \(R=(6,-1)\)

  • The line has a \(y\)-intercept of \((0,-7)\) and a slope of 1, so its equation is \(y=x−7\). \(Q\) and \(R\) are the points where the two functions have the same values, so \(x^2−8x+11=x−7\) at those points. Solving the equation gives \(x=6\) and \(x=3\).

\(\begin{array}{rcl}\;x^2-8x+11\;&=&x-7 \\ x^2-8x-x+11+7\;&=&0 \\ x^2-9x+18\;&=&0 \\ (x-6)(x-3)\;&=&0 \end{array}\)

\( x-6=0\) or \(x-3=0\)
\(\;\;\;\;\;\;x=6\) or \(x=3\)

Activity Synthesis

Invite one or more students to describe how they found their solutions in their own words, or if they got stuck, demonstrate the progress that they made.
