In all the division problems we completed that had a remainder of zero, we say that the divisor was a factor of the dividend.

Whenever a divisor, \(x-c\), divides a polynomial function, \(f(x)\), and results in a remainder of zero, we say \(x-c\) is a factor of \(f(x)\).

This leads us to the Factor Theorem.


FACTOR THEOREM

For any polynomial function \(f(x)\),

  • if \(x-c\) is a factor of \(f(x)\), then \(f(c)=0\).
  • if \(f(c)=0\), then \(x-c\) is a factor of \(f(x)\).

Use the Factor Theorem to determine whether the binomial is a factor of the given polynomial function. Be prepared to show your reasoning.


  1. Is \((x - 5)\) a factor of \(f(x)=x^3-125\)?

Answer: Yes, because \(f(5) = (5)^3 - 125 = 125 - 125 = 0\)

  1. Is \((x - 6)\) a factor of \(f(x)=x^3-216\)?

Answer: Yes, because \(f(6) = (6)^3 - 216 = 216 - 216 = 0\)

  1. Is \((x + 3)\) a factor of \(f(x)=x^3+8x^2+21x+18\)?

Answer: Yes, because \(f(-3) = (-3)^3 + 8(-3)^2 + 21(-3) + 18 = -27 + 72 - 63 + 18 = 0\)

  1. Is \((x + 4)\) a factor of \(f(x)=x^3+x^2-14x+8\)?

Answer: No, because \(f(-4) = (-4)^3 + (-4)^2 - 14(-4) + 8 = -64 + 16 + 56 + 8 = 16\)
