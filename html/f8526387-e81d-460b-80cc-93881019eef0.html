Warm Up (5 minutes)

This warm up refreshes students’ ability to convert a quadratic function from standard form to factored form. It also provides practice deriving the solutions, or zeros, of a function from its factored form. 

Students will use graphing technology to analyze the graph of a quadratic function by finding the \(x\)-intercepts and zeros.

Launch

Arrange students in groups of two. Ask students to work through the questions individually, but ask their partner if they have a question. 

Student Activity

  1. Write the quadratic function \(f(x) = x^2 − 9x + 20\) in factored form.

Answer: \(f(x) = (x − 4)(x − 5)\)

  1. What are the solutions of the quadratic equation \((x − 4)(x − 5) = 0\)?

Answer: \(x = 4\) and \(x = 5\)

  1. Graph the quadratic function \(f(x) = x^2− 9x + 20\). 

Use the Desmos graphing tool or technology outside the course. (Students were provided access to Desmos.)

What are the \(x\)-intercepts of the graph?

Answer: \((4, 0)\), \((5, 0)\) or 4 and 5

  1. Marcus states that the zeros of the function of \(f(x) = x^2 − 9x + 20\) are 4 and 5. Do you agree? Explain your reasoning.

Answer: Yes, 4 and 5 are the input values that result in an output of 0. When the function \(f(x) = x^2 − 9x + 20\) is graphed, it crosses the \(x\)-axis at \(x=4\) and \(x=5\).

Activity Synthesis

Ensure students understand moving from standard form to factored form to deriving solutions or zeros using a quadratic function. After students work through the questions, ask the class as a whole if there are any outstanding questions.

These are important skills that this lesson builds on as students learn more about writing a quadratic function when given its zeros.
