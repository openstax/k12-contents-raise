Activity (15 minutes)

Earlier in the lesson, students explored key features of the graphs of functions and what they mean in the context of a real life situation. Now, students will focus specifically on the graphs of linear functions. Students will identify \(x\)-intercepts as zeros, \(y\)-intercepts, and slopes.

Launch

Arrange students in groups of 2. Give students a few minutes of quiet time to think individually, and then time to discuss their response with their partner.

4.6.3: Self Check 

After the activity, students will answer the following question to check their understanding of the concepts explored in the activity. 

QUESTION: 

What is the zero of the linear function graphed below?

ANSWERS

FEEDBACK

\((6,0)\)

That’s correct! Check yourself: The zero is where the function crosses the \(x\)-axis.

\((0, -4)\)

Incorrect. Let’s try again a different way: This is the \(y\)-intercept. The zero is located where the function value is zero, at the \(x\)-intercept. The answer is \((6,0)\).

\((0,6)\)

Incorrect. Let’s try again a different way: The \(x\)-coordinate is the first value in the ordered pair, and the \(y\)-coordinate is the second value. The answer is \((6,0)\).

\((0, 0)\)

Incorrect. Let’s try again a different way: This is the origin, but it is not a point on the graph of this linear function. The answer is \((6,0)\).

Features of Linear Functions

The graph of a linear function forms a line that goes on forever in both directions. Since the ends go to infinity and negative infinity, linear functions do not have maximums or minimums, unless their domain is restricted to a specific part of the graph.

You can still pick out key features of linear functions.

The \(x\)-intercept, or zero, is where the line crosses the \(x\)-axis. This is where the \(y\)-coordinate is 0.

In this linear function, the \(x\)-intercept, or zero, is \(x= 2\) and written as the point \((2,0)\).

The \(y\)-intercept is where the line crosses the \(y\)-axis and the \(x\)-coordinate is 0.

In this linear function, the \(y\)-intercept is \(y=4\) and written as the point \((0,4)\).

The slope of the line can be found as in previous units as the change in \(y\) over the change in \(x\). 

\(\mathrm m=\;\frac{\mathrm{rise}}{\mathrm{run}}=\;\frac{\mathrm{change}\;\mathrm{in}\;\mathrm y}{\mathrm{change}\;\mathrm{in}\;\mathrm x}\)

The slope of this linear function is \(m= \frac{4}{-2}=\frac{-4}{2} =-2\).
