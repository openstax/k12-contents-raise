Activity (15 minutes)

This activity prompts students to contrast quantities that grow exponentially and quadratically by writing equations and creating tables of values. Before students begin working, they are asked to make an estimate of the number of squares in each pattern at Step 5 and Step 10. Making a reasonable estimate and comparing a computed value to one’s estimate is often an important aspect of making sense of problems. Later, from the tables, students notice that the output of the exponential function eventually outgrows that of the quadratic function. In the next activity, they will think further about whether this is always the case.

If students opt to use spreadsheet or graphing technology, they practice choosing appropriate tools strategically.

Launch

Display the image of the patterns for all to see and ask students to read the description of how the patterns grow. Ask students to predict which pattern will have more small squares in Step 5.

Then, ask students to predict which pattern will have more small squares in Step 10. Poll the class to collect their predictions. Display the number of students who think Pattern A will have more small squares and the number who think Pattern B will have more small squares.

Arrange students in groups of two. If time is limited, ask partners to each complete the questions for one pattern and then work together to compare the patterns and make observations.

Some students may choose to use a spreadsheet tool to study the pattern, and subsequently to use graphing technology to plot the data. Others may wish to use a calculator to compute growth factors. Provide access to devices that can run a spreadsheet tool, graphing technology, or a scientific calculator.

Anticipated Misconceptions

Some students may write the equation for Pattern B as \(g(n)=2n\). Point out that Pattern B is doubling the number of small squares. Step 3 would have \(4\) small squares. Prompt students to test their equation when \(n=3\) to see if it gives the correct output. \(g(3)=2(3)=6\), not \(4\), so a linear function does not work. Since Pattern B is doubling, the function is exponential, not linear. A linear pattern such as \(2n\) would add \(2\) small squares at each step rather than double the number of small squares.

Highlight the fact that a fundamental feature of an exponential function is that it changes by equal factors over equal intervals. In this exponential function, the output increases by a factor of \(2\) at each step.

In the quadratic function, we can see that the output changes by a factor of \(4\), then \(2\frac {1}{4}\), then \(1 \frac{7}{9}\), and so on. Even though it started out growing faster than the exponential function, the growth factor of the quadratic function decreases at each step and falls below \(2\) after a couple of steps. In the meantime, the growth factor of the exponential function stays at \(2\).

Also consider showing the graphs representing the two functions to help students visualize the data in the tables. This graph plots the outputs of \(f\) and \(g\) at whole-number inputs.

Discuss how the graphs representing both quadratic and exponential functions curve upward. The two are very close together for small values of \(x\). As \(x\) continues to grow, however, the values of \(g\) become much greater than those of \(f\) and continue to increase more quickly.

7.4.2: Self Check

After the activity, students will answer the following question to check their understanding of the concepts explored in the activity.

QUESTION:

Which of the following functions has a constant growth factor?

Answers Feedback
\(f(x)=4x\) Incorrect. Let’s try again a different way: The slope of this linear function is \(4\), creating a common difference of \(4\). The answer is \(f(x)=4^x\).
\(f(x)=4x^2\) Incorrect. Let’s try again a different way: Quadratic functions do not have a constant growth factor like exponential functions. The answer is \(f(x)=4^x\).
\(f(x)=4^x\) That’s correct! Check yourself: This exponential function has a growth factor of \(4\).
\(f(x)=x+4\) Incorrect. Let’s try again a different way: This linear function has a \(y\)-intercept of \(4\) but a slope or common difference of \(1\). The answer is \(f(x)=4^x\).
