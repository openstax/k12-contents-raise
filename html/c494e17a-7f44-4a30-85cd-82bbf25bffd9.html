Using Inequalities to Solve a Problem

Let’s look at an example where we might be able to solve a problem using inequalities.

A school is repainting its classroom walls. The school has at most 30 gallons of paint. The painter needs \(\frac52\) gallons of paint for each classroom. How many classrooms can the school paint at most?

We know that the answer is greater than or equal to 0 classrooms, so \(x\geq0\).

We also know that each classroom requires \(\frac52\) or 2.5 gallons of paint.

Let’s take a guess at how many classrooms and check if we are close to the solution. How many gallons of paint would we need to paint 10 classrooms?

\(10 × 2.5 = 25\), and since \(25 < 30\), it could be possible for the school to paint 10 classrooms.

We can try 11 classrooms. 

\(11 × 2.5 = 27.5\), and since \(27.5 < 30\), it could be possible for the school to paint 11 classrooms.

We can try 12 classrooms. 

\(12 × 2.5 = 30\), and since \(30 = 30\), the most classrooms the school could paint is 12 classrooms.

Try It: Using Inequalities to Solve a Problem

While working in a car factory, a worker can use a drill for as long as the batteries last. The worker has at most 9 fully charged batteries. Each car the worker helps assemble uses \(\frac34\) of a full battery. 

  1. What are all of the possible values for \(c\), the number of cars the worker can help assemble?

  2. Write one or more inequalities to represent your response. Be prepared to explain or Explain your reasoning.

Write down your answer. Then select the Solution button to check your work

Here is how to solve:

  1. \(c\leq12\) and \(c\geq0\). 

  2. If the worker has 9 fully charged batteries, they can help assemble 12 cars. If the worker has no batteries, they can assemble 0 cars. It is not possible for the worker to assemble less than 0 cars.
