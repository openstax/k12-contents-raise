Activity (20 minutes)

This activity introduces three different ways to multiply binomials together. Students should familiarize themselves with each method. Then they can decide which method works best for them and use the others to check their multiplication.

Launch

Digital Modeling Strategy - To model the use of algebra tiles, have students access the algebra tiles in Polypad at https://polypad.amplify.com/. Introduce the different types of tiles that students can access from the left side navigation bar.

  • The unit tile is the small yellow tile that has dimensions of 1 unit by 1 unit.
  • The linear tile, or \(“x”\) tile, has dimensions of 1 unit by \(x\) units.
  • The quadratic tile, or \(“x^2”\) tile, has dimensions of \(x\) units by \(x\) units.
  • Each of the tiles also have an additive inverse tile: \(-1\), \(-x\), and \(-x^2\).

    • As with the other methods, go step by step through the process using the algebra tiles. Begin by asking students to drag the algebra tiles needed for the first binomial into the work area. Circulate around the room to check which tiles students have selected. Watch that students have correctly pulled the units representing - 7. Next, have students line the tiles up with the variable tiles first and the unit tiles at the end. Repeat the process for assembling the terms for the second binomial.

    Be sure students line up one binomial as the length of a rectangle and the other as the width of the rectangle. The area of the rectangle they define represents the product of the binomials. Ask students to identify the terms one at a time and then drag the representation of the product into the area model. This process should appear similar to students since it mimics the process they used in the Unit's Inquiry Project. Use questions to scaffold the process such as the ones below.

    • What is the result of \(x \cdot x\)? What tile represents \(x^2\)?
    • What is the result of \(1 \cdot x\)? \(-1 \cdot 1\)? \(-1 \cdot x\)? etc. Which tile represents this product?

    Note that holding the “shift” key while selecting multiple tiles will create a group. A pop-up menu allows you to make a copy of what you have selected so that you can fill in terms more quickly. The pop-up menu also gives you the chance to negate a value if the wrong tile was selected.

    Students may have difficulty understanding why some of the terms have a negative value and why some are positive. Stress that this is an area model so the product is the result of the parts they identified along the length and width of the rectangle.

    Once the rectangle is complete, It is sometimes helpful to move the factors away from each side of the rectangle so students can focus on the product (resulting rectangle).

    Be sure to have students determine the simplified final product. It may be helpful to have students write out each of the similar terms separately, if a scaffold is needed. In this example, students may write \(2x^2 + 4x + -7x + -7x + 4(-7)\) before arriving at the final answer of \(2x^2 -10x - 28\).

    As a class, examine the three methods used in the table to multiply \((x-7)(2x+4)\). Go step by step through each method while asking for questions after each method. Then allow students to work through the exercises individually.

    When students use the Distributive Property, emphasize that the sign combining the new products is determined by the sign in the binomial that is broken up. The example from the table is shown below, with the corresponding signs in red:

    \((x\;{\style{color:red}-}\;7)(2x+4) = x(2x+4)\;{\style{color:red}-}\;7(2x+4)\)

    Activity Synthesis

    Ask students to explain which method they prefer. As students discuss each method, emphasize the following points:

    • To use the Distributive Property, the first binomial is broken up so that each term is multiplied by the second binomial.
    • When using Vertical Alignment, it is important to align like terms as you write the individual products. This will help you combine them into the correct final product.
    • When using the FOIL method, you can view the process as 4 individual steps that are then combined to find the product.

    Multiplying Binomials Using the Distributive Property

    Just like there are different ways to represent multiplication of numbers, there are several different ways to represent how to multiply a binomial times a binomial using the Distributive Property of Multiplication over Addition.

    Distribute to multiply the binomials.


    Example 1

    \((y+5)(y+8)\)

    Step 1 - Distribute the binomial \((y+8)\).

    \(y {\style{color:red}( }{\style{color:red}y}\;{\style{color:red}+}\;{\style{color:red}8}{\style{color:red}) }+ 5{\style{color:red}( }{\style{color:red}y}\;{\style{color:red}+}\;{\style{color:red}8}{\style{color:red}) }\)

    Step 2 - Now, distribute the individual terms.

    \(y^2+8y+5y+40\)

    Step 3 - Combine like terms.

    \(y^2+13y+40\)


    Example 2

    \((4y^2+3)(2y^2-5)\)

    Step 1 - Distribute the binomial \((2y^2 - 5)\).

    \(4y^2{\style{color:red}(}{\style{color:red}2}{\style{color:red}y}^{\style{color:red}2}{\style{color:red}−}{\style{color:red}5}{\style{color:red})}+ 3(2y^2−5) \)

    Step 2 - Distribute the individual terms.

    \(8y^2-20y^2+6y^2-15\)

    Step 3 - Combine like terms.

    \(8y^4-14y^2-15\)


    Multiplying Binomials Using the FOIL Method

    If you multiply binomials often enough, you may notice a pattern. Notice that the first term in the result is the product of the first terms in each binomial. The second and third terms are the product of multiplying the two outer terms and then the two inner terms. And the last term results from multiplying the two last terms.

    We abbreviate “First, Outer, Inner, Last” as FOIL. The letters stand for “First, Outer, Inner, Last.” We use this as another way to multiply binomials. The word FOIL is easy to remember and ensures we find all four products.

    Let’s multiply \((x+3)(x+7)\) using both methods.

    We summarize the steps of the FOIL method below. The FOIL method only applies to multiplying binomials, not other polynomials!

    How to use the FOIL method to multiply two binomials:

    Step 1 - Multiply the First terms.

    Step 2 - Multiply the Outer terms.

    Step 3 - Multiply the Inner terms.

    Step 4 - Multiply the Last terms.

    Step 5 - Combine like terms, when possible.


    When you multiply by the FOIL method, drawing the arrows will help your brain focus on the pattern and make it easier to apply.

    Now we will review examples where we use the FOIL pattern to multiply two binomials.

    Multiply the binomials using the FOIL method.

    Example 3

    \((y-7)(y+4)\)

    Step 1 - Multiply the \({\style{color:red}F}{\style{color:red}i}{\style{color:red}r}{\style{color:red}s}{\style{color:red}t}\) terms.

    Step 2 - Multiply the \({\style{color:red}O}{\style{color:red}u}{\style{color:red}t}{\style{color:red}e}{\style{color:red}r}\) terms.

    Step 3 - Multiply the \({\style{color:red}I}{\style{color:red}n}{\style{color:red}n}{\style{color:red}e}{\style{color:red}r}\) terms.

    Step 4 - Multiply the \({\style{color:red}L}{\style{color:red}a}{\style{color:red}s}{\style{color:red}t}\) terms.

    Step 5 - Combine like terms.

    \(y^2 - 3y - 28\)


    Example 4

    \((4x+3)(2x-5)\)

    Step 1 - Multiply the First terms, \( {\style{color:red}4} {\style{color:red}x} \; {\style{color:red}\cdot} \; {\style{color:red}2} {\style{color:red}x} \).

    Step 2 - Multiply the Outer terms, \( {\style{color:red}4} {\style{color:red}x}\; {\style{color:red}\cdot}\; {\style{color:red}(} {\style{color:red}{-5}} {\style{color:red})} \).

    Step 3 - Multiply the Inner terms, \( {\style{color:red}3}\; {\style{color:red}\cdot}\; {\style{color:red}2} {\style{color:red}x} \).

    Step 4 - Multiply the Last terms, \( {\style{color:red}3}\; {\style{color:red}\cdot}\; {\style{color:red}(} {\style{color:red}{-5}} {\style{color:red})} \).

    Step 5 - Combine like terms.
    \(8x^2-14x-15\)

    The FOIL method is usually the quickest method for multiplying two binomials, but it only works for binomials. You can use the Distributive Property to find the product of any two polynomials.

    Multiplying Binomials Using Vertical Alignment

    Another representation that works for all polynomials is Vertical Alignment. It is much like the method you use to multiply whole numbers. Look carefully at this example of multiplying two-digit numbers.

    \(\underline{\underline{\;\;\;\;23\\\times46}\\\;\;138\;\;\;\mathrm{partial}\;\mathrm{product}\\\;\;920\;\;\;\mathrm{partial}\;\mathrm{product}}\\1058\;\;\;\mathrm{product}\)

    Start by multiplying 23 by 6 to get 138.
    Next, multiply 23 by 4, lining up the partial product in the correct columns.
    Use a 0 as a place holder in the ones column because you will be multiplying by 4 tens.

    Finally, add the partial products.


    Example 5

    Multiply using Vertical Alignment: \((3y-1)(2y-6)\). It does not matter which binomial goes on the top.


    Step 1 - Multiply \(3y-1\) by \(-6\).

    Step 2 - Multiply \(3y-1\) by \(2y\).

    Step 3 - Add like terms.

    Notice the partial products are the same as the terms in the FOIL method.

    \(\begin{array}{l}\begin{array}{r}\;\;\left(3y-1\right)\\\underline{\times\left(2y-6\right)}\\{\style{color:red}-}\;{\style{color:red}1}{\style{color:red}8}{\style{color:red}y}\;{\style{color:red}+}\;{\style{color:red}6}\\{\style{color:blue}6}{\style{color:blue}y}^{\style{color:blue}2}\;{\style{color:blue}-}\;{\style{color:blue}2}{\style{color:blue}y}\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\\\hline6y^2-20y+6\end{array}\end{array}\)

    We have now used three ways to multiply binomials. Be sure to practice each process, and try to decide which one you prefer.
