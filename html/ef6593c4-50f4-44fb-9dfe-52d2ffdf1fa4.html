Activity (20 minutes)

This activity prompts students to simplify expressions using the Product and Quotient Properties for Exponents. Students must understand that to multiply like bases, the exponents are added and to divide like bases, the exponents are subtracted. The base in these situations always remains the same.

Launch

Read the Product Property written in the middle column. Review the example provided as a class. If needed, complete one additional row in the table as a class.

Limit the time spent on the first table to 10 minutes and pause the class for questions before students move on to the second table.

Then have students complete the second table. Read the Quotient Property written in the middle column. Review the example provided as a class. If needed, complete one additional row in the table as a class.

Product Property for Exponents

If \(a\) is a real number and \(m\) and \(n\) are integers, then

\(a^m \cdot a^n=a^{m+n}\)

To multiply with like bases, add the exponents.

Product Quotient for Exponents

If \(a\) is a real number, \(a\neq0\), and \(m\) and \(n\) are integers, then

\(\frac{a^m}{a^n}=a^{m-n}\), \(m>n\) and \(\frac{a^m}{a^n}=\frac1{a^{n-m}}\), \(n>m\)

To divide with like bases, subtract the exponents.

Activity Synthesis

Ask students to summarize the Product Property and Quotient Property for Exponents in their own words. Add any missing details as needed. Emphasize that when multiplying like bases, the exponents are added, when dividing like bases, the exponents are subtracted. Stress that the base in these situations always remains the same.

5.1.2: Additional Resources

Using Product and Quotient Properties for Exponents

When we combine like terms by adding and subtracting, we need to have the same base with the same exponent. But when you multiply and divide, the exponents may be different, and sometimes the bases may be different, too.

First, we will look at an example that leads to the Product Property.

What does \(x^2 \cdot x^3\) mean?


Notice that 5 is the sum of the exponents, 2 and 3. We see \(x^2 \cdot x^3\) is \(x^{2+3}\) or \(x^5\).

The base stayed the same, and we added the exponents. This leads to the Product Property for Exponents.


PRODUCT PROPERTY FOR EXPONENTS

If \(a\) is a real number and \(m\) and \(n\) are integers, then

\(a^m \cdot a^n=a^{m+n}\)

To multiply with like bases, add the exponents.


Now we will look at an exponent property for division. As before, we’ll try to discover a property by looking at some examples.

  1. Consider \(\frac{x^5}{x^2}\)

Step 1
What does it mean? \(\frac{x \cdot x \cdot x \cdot x \cdot x}{x \cdot x}\)

Step 2
Use the Equivalent Fractions Property.
x   ·   x   ·   x   ·   x   ·   x x   ·   x

Step 3
Simplify.
\(x^3\)

  1. Consider \(\frac{x^2}{x^3}\)

Step 1
What does it mean?
\(\frac{x \cdot x}{x \cdot x \cdot x}\)

Step 2
Use the Equivalent Fractions Property.
x   ·   x   ·   1 x   ·   x   ·   x

Step 3
Simplify.
\(\frac1x\)

Notice that in each case, the bases were the same and we subtracted exponents. We see \(\frac{x^5}{x^2}\) is \(x^{5-2}\) or \(x^3\). We see \(\frac{x^2}{x^3}\) is \(\frac1x\). When the larger exponent was in the numerator, we were left with factors in the numerator. When the larger exponent was in the denominator, we were left with factors in the denominator—notice the numerator of 1. When all the factors in the numerator have been removed, remember this is really dividing the factors to one, so we need a 1 in the numerator. x x   = 1 . This leads to the Quotient Property for Exponents.


QUOTIENT PROPERTY FOR EXPONENTS

If \(a\) is a real number, \(a\neq0\), and \(m\) and \(n\) are integers, then

\(\frac{a^m}{a^n}=a^{m-n}\), \(m>n\) and \(\frac{a^m}{a^n}=\frac1{a^{n-m}}\), \(n>m\)
