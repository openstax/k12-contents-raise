Activity (15 minutes)

This activity introduces students to factoring a trinomial of the form \(x^2 + bx + c\) by visualizing how to undo the FOIL process. Students will try to think of two numbers that are factors of \(c\) and have a sum of \(b\). The factors that meet these requirements will be the last terms of the binomial factors of the trinomial.

Launch

Solve the first example as a class. Pause on each question for students to answer. After students identify the correct factors, ask what should happen next. When students are able to correctly say they should be placed as the last terms of the two binomial factors, click the Solution button to reveal the factored form.

Then allow students to solve the problems individually. As students finish, ask if there are any questions.

Activity Synthesis

Emphasize to students that for “undoing FOIL” to be possible as a way to factor a polynomial, it must be a trinomial of the form \(x^2 + bx + c\). If the coefficient of the first term of degree \(2\) is not \(1\), then this is not an effective way to factor the polynomial.

When “undoing FOIL” to factor a trinomial, \(x^2 + bx + c\):

  • Think: What are the factors of \(c\)?
  • Which of these factors have a sum of \(b\)?
  • Use the factors that meet these requirements as the last terms of the binomial factors.
  • Multiply the binomials using FOIL to check your answer. The product should be the original trinomial.

How to Factor a Trinomial of the Form \(x^2 + bx + c\)

To figure out how we would factor a trinomial of the form \(x^2+bx+c\), such as \(x^2+5x+6\), and factor it to \((x+2)(x+3)\), let’s start with two general binomials of the form \((x+m)\) and \((x+n)\).

\((x+m)(x+n)\)

Step 1
FOIL to find the product.
\(x^2+mx+nx+mn\)

Step 2
Factor the GCF from the middle terms.
\(x^2+(m+n)x+mn\)

Our trinomial is of the form \(x^2+bx+c\).

This tells us that to factor a trinomial of the form \(x^2 + bx + c\), we need two factors \((x + m)\) and \((x + n)\) where the two numbers \(m\) and \(n\) multiply to \(c\) and add to \(b\).

EXAMPLE 1

Factor: \(x^2+11x+24\).

Step 1
Write the factors as two binomials with the first terms \(x\).
Write two sets of parentheses and put \(x\) as the first term.
\(x^2 + 11x + 24\)
\((x \;\;\;\; )(x \;\;\;\; )\)

Step 2
Find two numbers, \(m\) and \(n\), that

  • multiply to \(c\): \(m \cdot n = c\)
  • add to \(b\): \(m + n = b\)

Find two numbers that multiply to 24 and add to 11.

Let’s summarize the steps we used to find the factors.

How to factor trinomials of the form \(x^2 + bx + c\):

Step 1
Write the factors as two binomials with first terms x.
\(x^2\;+\;bx\;+\;c\\\;(x\;\;\;\;\;)(x\;\;\;\;\;)\)

Step 2
 Find two numbers, \(m\) and \(n\), that

  • multiply to \(c\): \(m \cdot n = c\)
  • add to \(b\): \(m + n = b\)

Step 3
Use \(m\) and \(n\) as the last terms of the factors.
\((x + m)(x + n)\)

Step 4
Check by multiplying the factors.

In the first example, all terms in the trinomial were positive. What happens when there are negative terms? Well, it depends which term is negative. Let’s look first at trinomials with only the middle term negative.

How do you get a positive product and a negative sum? We use two negative numbers.

Now, what if the last term in the trinomial is negative? Think about FOIL. The last term is the product of the last terms in the two binomials. A negative product results from multiplying two numbers with opposite signs. You have to be very careful when you choose factors to make sure you get the correct sign for the middle term, too. How do you get a negative product and a positive sum? We use one positive and one negative number. When we factor trinomials, we must have the terms written in descending order—in order from highest degree to lowest degree.

Sometimes you’ll need to factor trinomials of the form \(x^2 + bxy + cy^2\) with two variables, such as \(x^2 + 12xy + 36y^2\). The first term, \(x^2\), is the product of the first terms of the binomial factors, \(x \cdot x\). The \(y^2\) in the last term means that the second terms of the binomial factors must each contain \(y\). To get the coefficients \(b\) and \(c\), you use the same process summarized above.

EXAMPLE 4

Factor: \(r^2 − 8rs − 9s^2\).

We need \(r\) in the first term of each binomial and \(s\) in the second term. The last term of the trinomial is negative, so the factors must have opposite signs.

Step 1
Note that the first terms are \(r\) and the last terms contain \(s\).
\((r \;\;\;\; s)(r \;\;\;\; s)\)

Step 2
Find the numbers that multiply to −9 and add to −8.

\(1,-9\)

\(1+(−9)=−8 \checkmark \)

\(3,−3\)

\(3+(−3)=0\)


Step 3
Use 1 and –9 as coefficients of the last terms.
\((r+s)(r−9s)\)

Check.
\(\begin{array}{l}(r-9s)(r+s)\\r^2+rs-9rs-9s^2\\r^2-8rs-9s^2\end{array}\)

It is important to keep in mind that some trinomials are prime. The only way to be certain a trinomial is prime is to list all the possibilities and show that none of them work. If there are no factor pairs that fit all the requirements, then the trinomial is prime.

STRATEGY FOR FACTORING TRINOMIALS OF THE FORM \(x^2+bx+c\)

When we factor a trinomial, we look at the signs of its terms first to determine the signs of the binomial factors.
