Activity (20 minutes)

This activity prompts students to look at how the slope in slope-intercept form affects the graph of a linear function. Circulate to ensure students are graphing their lines properly. If necessary, partner students during the graphing process.

In pairs, students should discuss the vertical shifts to the parent function, \(f(x)=x\), to graph the new functions.

Launch

Allow students to work in pairs to check each other’s graphs and discuss the relationships to answer the questions. During class discussion, solicit a pair for each question to describe the effects the different slopes have on the graphs.

For students who can benefit from a more accessible model, use geoboards and rubber bands. Use one color rubber band for the \(x\)- and \(y\)-axes. Use a different color for each line graphed. Work with students to count spots on the geoboard and discuss the graphs and their relationships.

Vertical Stretches and Compressions

On the same coordinate grid graph the following linear functions:

  • Line a: \(f(x)=3x\)
  • Line b: \(f(x)=2x\)
  • Line c: \(f(x)=x\)

Graph Image: View Graph

  1. Question: What do you notice about the slopes of lines c, b, and a as the number of the coefficient gets larger?

    Answer: As the coefficient increases, the line gets steeper.

  2. Now graph lines d and e on the same graph as a-c:

    • Line d: \(f(x)= \frac12 x\)
    • Line e: \(f(x)= \frac13 x\)

    Graph Image: View Graph

    Question: How does line d representing \(f(x) = \frac12 x\) compare to line b representing \(f(x) = 2x\)?

    Answer: As the coefficient gets smaller, the line is less steep.

  3. Question: Examine lines d and e. What can you say about the lines when m<1?

    Answer: As the coefficient gets smaller, the line is less steep.

  4. Now graph lines f, g, and h on the same coordinate grid:

    • Line f: \(f(x)= -2x\)
    • Line g: \(f(x)= -x\)
    • Line h: \(f(x)= - \frac12 x\)

    Graph Image: View Graph

    Question: Compare the equations of lines f-h to the graphs of lines b-d. What changed with the equations of these functions?

    Answer: There is a negative coefficient in lines f-h while in lines b-d they are positive.

  5. Question: Compare the graphs of lines f-h to the graphs of lines b-d. How did the change in their equations affect their graphs?

    Answer: Since there is a negative coefficient, the graphs of lines f-h are reflections of the positive versions of the lines over the x-axis. The graphs were vertically “flipped.”

Activity Synthesis

Explain to students that graphing linear functions using transformations will assist them as you move to graphing different function families in the future.

Transformations of Linear Functions

Given the equation of a linear function, use transformations to graph the linear function in the form \(f(x)=mx+b\).

  1. Graph \(f(x)=x\).
  2. Vertically stretch or compress the graph by a factor \(m\).
  3. Shift the graph up or down \(b\) units.

Graph \(f(x)= \frac12 x−3\) using transformations.

The equation for the function shows that \(m=\frac12 \), so the identity function is vertically compressed by \(\frac12\). The equation for the function also shows that \(b=−3\), so the identity function is vertically shifted down 3 units. Now, show the vertical shift:

This graph shows two functions on an \((x, y)\) coordinate plane. The first is an increasing function of \(y = x\) divided by 2 and runs through the points \((0, 0)\) and \((2, 1)\). The second shows an increasing function of \(y = x\) divided by 2 minus 3 and passes through the points \((0, 3)\) and \((2, -2)\). An arrow pointing downward from the first function to the second function reveals the vertical shift.

Can we sketch a graph by reversing the order of the transformations?

No. The order of the transformations follows the order of operations. When the function is evaluated at a given input, the corresponding output is calculated by following the order of operations. So, the stretch was performed before the vertical shift.
