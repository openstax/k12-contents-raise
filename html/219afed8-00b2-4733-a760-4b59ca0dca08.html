  1. Here are graphs of functions \(f\) and \(g\).

Each represents the height of an object being launched into the air as a function of time.

    1. Which object was launched from a higher point?
      • The object represented by function \(f\)
      • The object represented by function \(g\)

    Answer: The object represented by function \(g\) because its \(y\)-intercept is above the \(y\)-intercept of the graph of \(f\).

    1. Which object reached a higher point?
      • The object represented by function \(f\)
      • The object represented by function \(g\)

    Answer: The object represented by function \(f\) because the vertex is higher.

    1. Which object was launched with the greater upward velocity?
      • The object represented by function \(f\)
      • The object represented by function \(g\)

    Answer: The object represented by function \(f\) because the “slope” near the \(y\)-intercept is greater. It increases in height much more quickly than the other object.

    1. Which object landed last?
      • The object represented by function \(f\)
      • The object represented by function \(g\)

    Answer: The object represented by function \(g\) because the positive \(x\)-intercept of its graph is to the right of the positive \(x\)-intercept of the graph of \(f\).

  1. The function \(h\) given by \(h(t)=(1−t)(8+16t)\) models the height of a ball in feet, \(t\) seconds after it was thrown.
    1. Find one of the zeros of the functions using \((8+16t)\).

    Answer: -0.5. From the factored form, we can tell that the graph intersects the horizontal axis at \(t=-0.5\). The zero of the function is the same value.

    1. Find one of the zeros of the functions using \((1-t)\).

    Answer: 1. From the factored form, we can tell that the graph intersects the horizontal axis at \((1-t)\). The zero of the function is the same value.

    1. What do the zeros tell us in this situation?
      • The positive zero means the time, in seconds, when the basketball is on the ground or hits the ground.
      • The negative zero represents the height, in feet, when the time is zero.
      • The positive zero represents the time, in seconds, when the basketball reaches the maximum height.
      • The negative zero represents the time, in seconds, when the basketball is on the ground or hits the ground.

    Answer: The positive zero means the time, in seconds, when the basketball is on the ground or hits the ground.

    1. Are both zeros meaningful?
      • Yes
      • No

    Answer: No. Only the positive zero is meaningful. It tells us the ball hits the ground after 1 second.

    1. From what height, in feet, is the ball thrown?

    Answer: 8 feet. When \((1−t)(8+16t)\)is rewritten in standard form it is \(8+8t−16t^2\). The constant term (which corresponds to the \(y\)-intercept) tells us the height of the basketball when \(t=0\) or when it is launched.

    1. When, in seconds, does the ball reach its highest point?

    Answer: 0.25 seconds. Graphing \(h\) shows the maximum point at \((0.25,9)\). Or, you could recognize that 0.25 is the \(x\)-coordinate of the vertex because it is halfway between the two zeros, -0.5 and 1.

    1. How high, in feet, does the ball go?

    Answer: 9 feet. Graphing \(h\) shows the maximum point at \((0.25,9)\). Or, you could recognize that 0.25 is the \(x\)-coordinate of the vertex because it is halfway between the two zeros, -0.5 and 1. Then evaluate the function at 0.25 to get a height of 9.

  1. The height in feet of a thrown football is modeled by the equation \(f(t)=6+30t−16t^2\), where time \(t\) is measured in seconds.
    1. Which term in the equation describes how many feet above the ground the football was thrown?
      • The constant term 6
      • The linear term \(30t\)
      • The squared term \(-16t^2\)
      • The squared term \(16t^2\)

    Answer: The constant term 6

    1. Which term in the equation describes the initial upward velocity of the football in feet per second?
      • The constant term 6
      • The linear term \(30t\)
      • The squared term \(-16t^2\)
      • The squared term \(16t^2\)

    Answer: The linear term \(30t\).

    1. How does the squared term \(-16t^2\) affect the value of the function \(f\)?
      • The squared term decreases the value of the function.
      • The squared term increases the value of the function.
      • The squared term has no effect on the value of the function.
      • The value of the function is zero when \(-16t^2\) is zero.

    Answer: The squared term decreases the value of the function because the values of \(16t^2\) are being subtracted from \(6+30t\)

    1. What does this term \(−16t^2\) reveal about the situation?
      • The influence of gravity pulling the ball down to the ground.
      • The initial upward velocity of the football.
      • How far the football was thrown above the ground.
      • How long the ball is in the air before it hits the ground.

    Answer: The influence of gravity pulling the ball down to the ground.

  1. The height in feet of an arrow is modeled by the equation \(h(t)=(1+2t)(18−8t)\), where \(t\) is seconds after the arrow is shot.
    1. At what time, in seconds, does the arrow hit the ground?

    Answer: 2.25. The positive zero is 2.25 because \((18−8t)\) is equal to zero when \(t=2.25\). The zeros mean the times, in seconds, when the arrow is on the ground. Only the positive zero makes sense. The negative zero would represent a time before the arrow was launched.

    1. At what height, in feet, is the arrow shot?

    Answer: 18 feet. When the arrow is shot, \(t\) is 0 and \(h(0)=(1+2⋅0)(18−8⋅0)=(1)(18)=18\).

5. Two objects are launched into the air.

The height, in feet, of Object A is given by the equation \(f(t)=4+32t−16t^2\).

The height, in feet, of Object B is given by the equation \(g(t)=2.5+40t−16^2\).

In both functions, \(t\) is seconds after launch.

    1. Which object was launched from a greater height?
      • Object A
      • Object B

    Answer: Object A. The objects are launched when \(t=0\). \(f(0)=4+32⋅0−16(0)2=4\) and \(g(0)=2.5+40⋅0−16(0)2=2.5\).

    1. Which object was launched with a greater upward velocity?
      • Object A
      • Object B

    Answer: Object B. The coefficient of \(t\) gives the upward velocity and 40 is greater than 32.
