Solving Quadratic Equations of the Form \(a(x - h)^2 = k\)

We can use the square root property to solve an equation of the form \(a(x-h)^2=k\) as well.

Notice that the quadratic term, \(x\), in the original form \(ax^2=k\) is replaced with \((x−h)\).

\(a {\style{color:red}x}^{2}=k \quad a{\style{color:red}(}{\style{color:red}x}\;{\style{color:red}-}\;{\style{color:red}h}{\style{color:red})}^{2}=k\)

Like before, the first step is to isolate the term that has the variable squared. In this case, a binomial is being squared.

Once the binomial is isolated, by dividing each side by the coefficient of \(a\), the square root property can be used on \((x−h)^2\).

Solve \(4(y−7)^2=48\).

Step 1
Isolate the quadratic term and make its coefficient 1. So, divide both sides of the equation by 4.

\(4(y−7)^2=48\)
\((y−7)^2=12\)

Step 2
Use the square root property on the binomial.
\(y−7= \pm \sqrt{12}\)

Step 3
Simplify the radical by pulling out the factors that are perfect squares.
Solve for \(y\). Rewrite the two solutions.

\(y−7= \pm \sqrt{12}\)
\(y−7= \pm \sqrt{4 \cdot 3}\)
\(y−7= \pm 2 \sqrt{3}\)
\(y =7 \pm 2 \sqrt{3}\)
\(y =7+2 \sqrt{3}\), \(y=7−2 \sqrt{3}\)

Step 4
Check the solutions.

\(\begin{array}{rrr} 4(y-7)^{2}=48 & 4(y-7)^{2}=48 \\ 4({\style{color:red}7}\;{\style{color:red}+}\;{\style{color:red}2} \sqrt{{\style{color:red}3}}-7)^{2} \stackrel{?}{=} 48 & 4({\style{color:red}7}\;{\style{color:red}-}\;{\style{color:red}2} \sqrt{{\style{color:red}3}}-7)^{2} \stackrel{?}{=} 48 \\ 4(2 \sqrt{3})^{2} \stackrel{?}{=} 48 & 4(-2 \sqrt{3})^{2} \stackrel{?}{=} 48 \\ 4(12)^{2} \stackrel{?}{=} 48 & 4(12)^{2} \stackrel{?}{=} 48 \\ 48=48 {\style{color:red}\checkmark} & 48=48 {\style{color:red}\checkmark} \end{array} \)

Try It: Solving Quadratic Equations of the Form \(a(x-h)^2=k\)

Solve the following quadratic equations.

  1. \(3(a−3)^2=363\)
  1. \(\frac{1}{2} (b+2)^2=200\)

Write down your answers, then select the solution button to compare your work.

Here is how to solve these quadratic equations of the form \(a(x-h)^2=k\):

  1. \(3(a−3)^2 =363\)
    \((a−3)^2=121\)
    \(a−3= \pm 11\)
    \(a−3=11\)      \(a−3=-11\)
    \(a= 14\) and \(a=-8\)
  1. \(\frac{1}{2}(b+2)^2=200\)
    \((b+2)^2=400\)
    \(b+2= \pm 20\)
    \(b+2=20\)      \(b+2=-20\)
    \(b=18\) and \(b=-22\)
