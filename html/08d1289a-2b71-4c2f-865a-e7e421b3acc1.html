Throughout each unit, we have provided a core sampling of teaching strategies that align to the English Language Proficiency Standards. The alignments are not intended to be all inclusive. They are to demonstrate a sampling of ways to support English language learners within the math classroom. Each lesson offers ELPS aligned to at least three different domains and across the entire unit, all five ELPS domains are represented and aligned. You can pull these strategies for learning, listening, speaking, reading, and writing for any of the lessons. To see examples of teaching routines for each standard, please view the Teacher Guide for Course Level Resources.

ELPS Sample Math Routines
ELPS Standards
(1) Cross-curricular second language acquisition/learning strategies. The ELL uses language learning strategies to develop an awareness of his or her own learning processes in all content areas. In order for the ELL to meet grade-level learning expectations across the foundation and enrichment curriculum, all instruction delivered in English must be linguistically accommodated (communicated, sequenced, and scaffolded) commensurate with the student's level of English language proficiency. The student is expected to:
(1A) use prior knowledge and experiences to understand meanings in English; Vocabulary: Incorporate into vocabulary definitions and explanations in a warm up activity. 
(1B) monitor oral and written language production and employ self-corrective techniques or other resources; Speaking and Writing: Provide accountable conversation opportunities for students to interact with peers and revisit work, self-correct language errors, and expand language.
(1C) use strategic learning techniques such as concept mapping, drawing, memorizing, comparing, contrasting, and reviewing to acquire basic and grade-level vocabulary; Vocabulary: Incorporate opportunities for students to create visual representations, gestures, songs, mnemonic devices to add meaning to new vocabulary.
(1D) speak using learning strategies such as requesting assistance, employing non-verbal cues, and using synonyms and circumlocution (conveying ideas by defining or describing when exact English words are not known); Speaking: Provide sentence stems and/or visual support within the classroom and opportunities for students to share and expand their language.
(1E) internalize new basic and academic language by using and reusing it in meaningful ways in speaking and writing activities that build concept and language attainment; Speaking and Writing: Provide accountable conversation opportunities for students to interact with peers and revisit work, self-correct language errors, and expand language.
(1F) use accessible language and learn new and essential language in the process; Speaking and Writing: Provide accountable conversation opportunities for students to interact with peers and revisit work, self-correct language errors, and expand language.
(1G) demonstrate an increasing ability to distinguish between formal and informal English and an increasing knowledge of when to use each one commensurate with grade-level learning expectations; and Speaking and Writing: Provide accountable conversation opportunities for students to interact with peers using informal/formal language and expand both written and oral language.
(1H) develop and expand repertoire of learning strategies such as reasoning inductively or deductively, looking for patterns in language, and analyzing sayings and expressions commensurate with grade-level learning expectations. Speaking and Writing: Provide accountable conversation opportunities for students to interact with peers with a focus on patterns within language and expand both written and oral language.
(2) Cross-curricular second language acquisition/listening. The ELL listens to a variety of speakers including teachers, peers, and electronic media to gain an increasing level of comprehension of newly acquired language in all content areas. ELLs may be at the beginning, intermediate, advanced, or advanced high stage of English language acquisition in listening. In order for the ELL to meet grade-level learning expectations across the foundation and enrichment curriculum, all instruction delivered in English must be linguistically accommodated (communicated, sequenced, and scaffolded) commensurate with the student's level of English language proficiency. The student is expected to:
(2A) distinguish sounds and intonation patterns of English with increasing ease; Vocabulary: Incorporate teacher questions and provide sentence stems to students focusing on intonation sounds. Ask questions like, “Did I ask a question or make a statement?”, “How did you know?”
(2B) recognize elements of the English sound system in newly acquired vocabulary such as long and short vowels, silent letters, and consonant clusters; Vocabulary: Incorporate a classroom word wall and word sorts into your lessons. Focus students on sounds within key vocabulary words and phrases.
(2C) learn new language structures, expressions, and basic and academic vocabulary heard during classroom instruction and interactions; Vocabulary: Incorporate a classroom word wall and word sorts into your lessons. Focus students on sounds within key vocabulary words and phrases.
(2D) monitor understanding of spoken language during classroom instruction and interactions and seek clarification as needed; Partner & Group Collaboration: Provide opportunities for teacher/peer interactions to repeat and rephrase key concepts of the lesson. 
(2E) use visual, contextual, and linguistic support to enhance and confirm understanding of increasingly complex and elaborated spoken language; Vocabulary: Incorporate a classroom word wall and word sorts into your lessons. Focus students on sounds within key vocabulary words and phrases.
(2F) listen to and derive meaning from a variety of media such as audiotape, video, DVD, and CD-ROM to build and reinforce concept and language attainment; Writing: Ask students to take notes from teacher demonstrations or videos to capture the general meaning, main points, and key details. Provide a summarization frame if additional support is needed.
(2G) understand the general meaning, main points, and important details of spoken language ranging from situations in which topics, language, and contexts are familiar to unfamiliar; Writing: Ask students to take notes from teacher demonstrations or videos to capture the general meaning, main points, and key details. Provide a summarization frame for additional support.
(2H) understand implicit ideas and information in increasingly complex spoken language commensurate with grade-level learning expectations; and Writing: Ask students to take notes from teacher demonstrations or video to capture general meaning, main points, and key details. The student will identify implicit ideas and information heard in discussion by participating in a Carousel Sharing Activity.
(2I) demonstrate listening comprehension of increasingly complex spoken English by following directions, retelling or summarizing spoken messages, responding to questions and requests, collaborating with peers, and taking notes commensurate with content and grade-level needs. Partner & Group Collaboration: Ask students to take notes from teacher demonstrations or videos to capture general meaning, main points, and key details. Provide opportunities to respond to questions independently or within predetermined peer groups. Provide sentence stems to increase the complexity of shared language.
(3) Cross-curricular second language acquisition/speaking. The ELL speaks in a variety of modes for a variety of purposes with an awareness of different language registers (formal/informal) using vocabulary with increasing fluency and accuracy in language arts and all content areas. ELLs may be at the beginning, intermediate, advanced, or advanced high stage of English language acquisition in speaking. In order for the ELL to meet grade-level learning expectations across the foundation and enrichment curriculum, all instruction delivered in English must be linguistically accommodated (communicated, sequenced, and scaffolded) commensurate with the student's level of English language proficiency. The student is expected to:
(3A) practice producing sounds of newly acquired vocabulary such as long and short vowels, silent letters, and consonant clusters to pronounce English words in a manner that is increasingly comprehensible; Partner & Group Collaboration: Incorporate teacher questions and provide sentence stems for students. Allow students opportunities to practice producing sounds for key vocabulary as they demonstrate/extend their understanding. Example: The letter ____ in the word _____ is long because …
(3B) expand and internalize initial English vocabulary by learning and using high-frequency English words necessary for identifying and describing people, places, and objects, by retelling simple stories and basic information represented or supported by pictures, and by learning and using routine language needed for classroom communication; Partner & Group Collaboration: Provide accountable conversation and writing opportunities for students to interact with peers and solve or create real-world story problems. Support students by providing high-frequency word lists and/or visuals to guide and extend their work.
(3C) speak using a variety of grammatical structures, sentence lengths, sentence types, and connecting words with increasing accuracy and ease as more English is acquired; Partner & Group Collaboration: Provide accountable conversation and writing opportunities for students to interact with peers and solve or create real-world story problems. Support students by providing sentence stems with specific grammatical structures and/or visuals to guide and extend their work. Example: Where is/are…?
(3D) speak using grade-level content area vocabulary in context to internalize new English words and build academic language proficiency; Vocabulary: Have students work in predetermined pairs/groups and discuss their work, being sure to encourage use of content-specific vocabulary. Add vocabulary to class word wall or student notebooks.
(3E) share information in cooperative learning interactions; Partner & Group Collaboration: Have students write or share orally their steps when solving problems. In predetermined groups or pairs, students share their steps and agree/disagree with the step and can work cooperatively to modify responses and resolve if needed.
(3F) ask and give information ranging from using a very limited bank of high-frequency, high-need, concrete vocabulary, including key words and expressions needed for basic communication in academic and social contexts, to using abstract and content-based vocabulary during extended speaking assignments; Vocabulary: Have students work in predetermined pairs/groups and discuss their work, being sure to encourage use of content-specific vocabulary. Add vocabulary to class word wall or student notebooks and provide vocabulary and sentence stems as needed to extend student’s language.
(3G) express opinions, ideas, and feelings ranging from communicating single words and short phrases to participating in extended discussions on a variety of social and grade-appropriate academic topics; Partner & Group Collaboration: Have students write or share orally their steps when solving problems. In predetermined groups or pairs, students share their steps and agree/disagree with the step and can work cooperatively to modify responses and resolve if needed.
(3H) narrate, describe, and explain with increasing specificity and detail as more English is acquired; Partner & Group Collaboration: Have students write or share orally their steps when solving problems. In predetermined groups or pairs, students share their steps and agree/disagree with the step and can work cooperatively to modify responses and resolve if needed.
(3I) adapt spoken language appropriately for formal and informal purposes; and Speaking and Writing: Provide accountable conversation opportunities for students to interact with peers using informal/formal language and expand both written and oral language.
(3J) respond orally to information presented in a wide variety of print, electronic, audio, and visual media to build and reinforce concept and language attainment. Speaking and Writing: Provide accountable conversation opportunities for students to interact with peers using informal/formal language and expand both written and oral language.
(4) Cross-curricular second language acquisition/reading. The ELL reads a variety of texts for a variety of purposes with an increasing level of comprehension in all content areas. ELLs may be at the beginning, intermediate, advanced, or advanced high stage of English language acquisition in reading. In order for the ELL to meet grade-level learning expectations across the foundation and enrichment curriculum, all instruction delivered in English must be linguistically accommodated (communicated, sequenced, and scaffolded) commensurate with the student's level of English language proficiency. For Kindergarten and Grade 1, certain of these student expectations apply to text read aloud for students not yet at the stage of decoding written text. The student is expected to:
(4A) learn relationships between sounds and letters of the English language and decode (sound out) words using a combination of skills such as recognizing sound-letter relationships and identifying cognates, affixes, roots, and base words; Vocabulary: Incorporate direct teaching of cognates, affixes, roots, and base words when appropriate to the lesson. Add to your class or student word walls.
(4B) recognize directionality of English reading such as left to right and top to bottom; Word Problems: Guide students during lessons that involve real-world word problems especially within 1:1 support. 
(4C) develop basic sight vocabulary, derive meaning of environmental print, and comprehend English vocabulary and language structures used routinely in written classroom materials; Vocabulary: Incorporate into vocabulary definitions and explanations in a warm up activity. 
(4D) use prereading supports such as graphic organizers, illustrations, and pretaught topic-related vocabulary and other prereading activities to enhance comprehension of written text; Vocabulary: Incorporate into vocabulary definitions and explanations when introducing word walls, using warm up activities, comparing polysemous words, etc. Example: Have students respond to lesson images using the stem: "This picture is about …” 
(4E) read linguistically accommodated content area material with a decreasing need for linguistic accommodations as more English is learned; Writing: Provide alternate questions that remove extraneous information, replace word numbers with numerals and simplify the overall language.

(5) Cross-curricular second language acquisition/writing. The ELL writes in a variety of forms with increasing accuracy to effectively address a specific purpose and audience in all content areas. ELLs may be at the beginning, intermediate, advanced, or advanced high stage of English language acquisition in writing. In order for the ELL to meet grade-level learning expectations across foundation and enrichment curriculum, all instruction delivered in English must be linguistically accommodated (communicated, sequenced, and scaffolded) commensurate with the student's level of English language proficiency. For Kindergarten and Grade 1, certain of these student expectations do not apply until the student has reached the stage of generating original written text using a standard writing system. The student is expected to:
(5A) learn relationships between sounds and letters of the English language to represent sounds when writing in English; Writing: Incorporate into journaling reflections by emphasizing/correcting/encouraging the use of newly acquired vocabulary and relationship of letters and sounds. Provide sentence stems or cloze sentences that will aid in extending student language acquisition. Consider adding words to a class word wall.

Word Problems: Provide feedback on writing as a part of explanation-based student responses. 
(5B) write using newly acquired basic vocabulary and content-based grade-level vocabulary; Writing: Incorporate into journaling reflections by emphasizing/correcting/encouraging the use of newly acquired vocabulary. Provide sentence stems or cloze sentences that will aid in extending student language acquisition. Consider adding words to a class word wall.

Word Problems: Provide feedback on writing as a part of explanation-based student responses. 
(5C) spell familiar English words with increasing accuracy, and employ English spelling patterns and rules with increasing accuracy as more English is acquired; Writing: Incorporate into journaling reflections by emphasizing/correcting/encouraging the use of correct grammatical structures and spellings. Consider adding words to a class word wall. Word Problems: Provide feedback on writing as a part of explanation-based student responses. 
(5D) edit writing for standard grammar and usage, including subject-verb agreement, pronoun agreement, and appropriate verb tenses commensurate with grade-level expectations as more English is acquired; Word Problems: Incorporate into journaling reflections by emphasizing/correcting/encouraging the use of correct grammatical structures. Consider adding words to a class word wall or creating word sorts. 
(5E) employ increasingly complex grammatical structures in content area writing commensurate with grade-level expectations, such as: Word Problems: Incorporate into journaling reflections by emphasizing/correcting/encouraging the use of correct grammatical structures. 
(5Ei) using correct verbs, tenses, and pronouns/antecedents; Word Problems: Incorporate into journaling reflections by emphasizing/correcting/encouraging the use of correct grammatical structures. 
(5Eii) using possessive case (apostrophe s) correctly; and Word Problems: Incorporate into journaling reflections by emphasizing/correcting/encouraging the use of correct grammatical structures. 
(5Eiii) using negatives and contractions correctly. Word Problems: Incorporate into journaling reflections by emphasizing/correcting/encouraging the use of correct grammatical structures.
