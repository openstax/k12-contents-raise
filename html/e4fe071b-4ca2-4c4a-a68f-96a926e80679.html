Activity (10 minutes)

In this activity, students compare graphs and statements that represent functions without a context. Because no concrete information is given, students need to rely on their understanding of function notation and points on a graph to make comparisons and to interpret intersections of the graphs.

Previously, students saw equations such as \(f(8)=g(8)\) and interpreted them in terms of a situation. Here, they begin to reason more abstractly about statements of the form \(f(x)=g(x)\) and relate it to one or more points where the graphs of \(f\) and \(g\) intersect. They see that a value of \(x\) that makes this equation true, or a solution to the equation, is the input value of such an intersection.

Student Facing Statement

Here are graphs that represent two functions, \(f\) and \(g\).

  1. Decide which function value is greater for each given input. Be prepared to explain your reasoning.
    1. \(f(2)\) or \(g(2)\)
    2. \(f(4)\) or \(g(4)\)
    3. \(f(6)\) or \(g(6)\)
    4. \(f(8)\) or \(g(8)\)

    2. ANSWER:

    1. \(g(2)\) is greater.
    2. They appear to be equal.
    3. \(f(6)\) is greater.
    4. \( f(8)\) is greater.

  2. Is there a value of \(x\) at which the equation \(f(x)=g(x)\) is true? Be prepared to show your reasoning.

    3. ANSWER: Yes. For example: If the graphs intersect when \(x\) is 4 and when it is 9, then \(f(4)=g(4)\) and \(f(9)=g(9)\).

  3. Identify at least two values of \(x\) at which the inequality \(f(x)<g(x)\) is true.

    4. ANSWER: For example: 1, 3, 10, 11, or other \(x\)-values that are less than 4 and greater than 9

Activity Synthesis

Display the graphs of \(f\) and \(g\) for all to see. Invite students to share their responses to the first set of questions. As they point out the greater function value in each pair, mark the point on the graph and write a corresponding statement in function notation:

\(f(2)<g(2)\), \(f(4)=g(4)\), \(f(6)>g(6)\), and \(f(8)>g(8)\). Emphasize that the function value that is greater in each pair has a higher vertical value on the coordinate plane for the same input value.

Next, discuss if there are values of \(x\) that make \(f(x)=g(x)\) true and how we can tell. Point out that earlier we wrote \(f(4)=g(4)\) because the values of (f\) and \(g\) are equal when the input is 4. This means that \((4,f(4))\) and \((4,g(4))\) are coordinates of the same point.

So, we can interpret an equation such as \(f(x)=g(x)\) to mean that the values of \(f\) and \(g\) are equal when the input is \(x\), and that \(x\) must be the horizontal value of the intersection of both graphs, which is a point that they share.

4.9.4: Self Check

After the activity, students will answer the following question to check their understanding of the concepts explored in the activity.

QUESTION:

Which of the following is true about the graphs of \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) below?

ANSWERS

FEEDBACK

\(f(0)>g(0)\)

Incorrect. Let’s try again a different way: The graph of \(g\) is higher than the graph of \(f\) at \(x=0\). The answer is \(f(1)=g(1)\).

\(f(1)=g(1)\)

That’s correct! Check yourself: When \(x=1\), both \(f\) and \(g\) equal –3.

\(f(-3)<g(-3)\)

Incorrect. Let’s try again a different way: At \(x = -3\), \(f\) is higher than \(g\). The answer is \(f(1)=g(1)\).

\(f(-1)=g(-1)\)

Incorrect. Let’s try again a different way: At \(x =-1\), \(g\) is greater than \(f\). The answer is \(f(1)=g(1)\).

4.9.4: Additional Resources

The following content is available to students who would like more support based on their experience with the self check. Students will not automatically have access to this content, so you may wish to share it with those who could benefit from it.

Using Graphs to Find Values

Two functions, \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\), are graphed below.

What is the value of \(f(-1)\)?

Look at where \(x=-1\) touches the graph of \(f\). The \(y\)-value is –3.

\(f(-1)=-3\)

What is the value of \(g(-1)\)?

Look where \(x=-1\) touches the graph of \(g\). The \(y\)-value is –1.

\(g(-1)=-1\)

Which value is greater?

\(g(-1)>f(-1)\)

Try It: Using Graphs to Find Values

Two functions, \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\), are graphed below.

Which is greater, \(f(1)\) or \(g(1)\)?

Write down your answer. Then select the Solution button to compare your work.

Solution

Here is how to determine which value is greater:

Looking at the graph, \(f(1)\) is where \(x=1\) reaches the graph of \(f(x)\). The \(y\)-value there is 4. So, \(f(1)=4\).

Similarly, \(g(1)\) is where \(x=1\) reaches the graph of \(g(x)\). The \(y\)-value there is 2.

So, \(f(1) >g(1)\).
