Here are some key takeaways from this lesson to share with students:

  • An essential step in finding the GCF of two or more expressions is writing each of the expressions in expanded form as the multiplication of all of its prime factors.
  • To find the GCF of two or more expressions, list all the factors of each expression. Then find the common factors that are in every expression. Multiply these factors together.
  • To factor the GCF from a polynomial, first find the GCF of all of the terms in the polynomial. Then use the “reverse” of the Distributive Property to factor the GCF out of each expression in the polynomial.
  • Sometimes there is not a common factor in every term of a polynomial. However, it can still be factored using a method called grouping. The terms of the polynomial are grouped together so that a GCF can be factored from the first part and another GCF can be factored from the second part. If the polynomial can be factored further, a common factor will emerge from both parts.
