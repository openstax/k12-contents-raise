Warm Up (5 minutes)

This warm up reviews perfect squares and square roots and begins to elicit the idea of higher powers and the nth root.

Launch

Arrange students in groups of 2.

Student Activity

  1. What is the value of \(4 \times 4\)?

    Answer: 16
  1. What is the value of \(4^2\)?

    Answer: 16
  1. Using what you know about roots, what is the value of \(\sqrt{16}\)?

    Answer: 4
  1. Talk to a partner. How can you prove \(\sqrt[2]{4^2}\;=\;4\)?

    Answer: Since \(4^2\;=\;4\;\cdot\;4\;=\;16\) and \(\sqrt{16}\;=\;4\), \(\sqrt[2]{4^2}\;=\;4\).

    Notice that the root and exponents are the same and simplify to leave the integer only.

  1. Talk to a partner. What do you think the answer to \(\sqrt[5]{4^5}\) would be?

    Answer: \(\sqrt[5]{4^5}\;=\;4\)

Activity Synthesis

If time allows, continue to give more examples where the exponent and index of the root are the same. Ask students to come up with a rule or pattern.
