Find the \(x\)-Intercepts

The \(x\)-intercepts of the graph of an equation occur when \(y = 0\).

Example 1

For the equation \(y=(x-9)(x+2)\), look at the factors \((x-9)\) and \((x+2)\).
Notice in the first factor, \((x-9)\), \(x=9\) makes the factor equal to 0.
Substitute 9 for \(x\) in the equation.

\(y=(9-9)(9+2)\)
\(y=(0)(11)\)
\(y=0\)

Notice in the second factor, \((x+2)\), \(x=-2\) makes the factor equal to 0.
Substitute -2 for \(x\) in the equation.

\(y=(-2-9)(-2+2)\)
\(y=(-11)(0)\)
\(y=0\)

In the equation \(y=(x-9)(x+2)\), \(y=0\) when \(x=9\) and \(x=-2\).
The \(x\)-intercepts occur when \(x=9\) and \(x=-2\).
To find the \(x\)-intercepts of a quadratic equation in factored form, set each factor equal to zero and solve for \(x\).

Example 2

Find the \(x\)-intercepts for \(y=(2x+7)(x-4)\).

The \(x\)-intercepts occur when \(y = 0\).
Set each factor to 0 and solve.

\(\begin{array}{rcl}2x+7&=&0\;\;\;\;\;x-4&=&0\\x&=&-3.5\;\;\;\;\;\;x&=&4\end{array}\)

Here is how to find the \(x\)-intercepts:

The \(x\)-intercepts occur when \(y = 0\).
Set each factor to 0 and solve.

\(2x-5 =0\;\;\;\;x+3=0\)
\(x = 2.5\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;x = –3\)

\(\begin{array}{rcl}2x-5&=&0\;\;\;\;\;x+3&=&0\\x&=&2.5\;\;\;\;\;\;x&=&-3\end{array}\)
