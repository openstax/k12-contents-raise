Activity (20 minutes)

Students have already learned one way to factor trinomials of the form \(ax^2 + bx + c\). In this activity, they will be introduced to the “\(ac\)” method, which works on any trinomial of the form \(ax^2 + bx + c\).

Students will also learn to recognize situations where they can substitute the variable \(u\) for an expression, which leaves them a trinomial of the form \(ax^2 + bx + c\), which is much more recognizable to factor.

Launch

Open the activity by introducing the “\(ac\)” method to students. Explain that this method will allow them to factor any trinomial of the form \(ax^2 + bx + c\). Tell students that while this is a new way to factor trinomials, it is based on skills that they have already learned. Read through each step, stopping to address any confusion as needed.

Keep the steps visible to the class and work through the first problem. This will help solidify the process using the “\(ac\)” method. If students are feeling less confident in the process, allow them to work in pairs as they complete questions 2–3. This will encourage them to talk out the process as they learn a new method to factor trinomials.

As a class, read the text on factoring by substitution that precedes question 4. To provide a hint for question 4, display the equation \(u = y^2\). By using this equation to substitute, students will find a trinomial of the form \(x^2 + bx + c\) that they will recognize.

Allow time for students to complete questions 4–6. Pause for student questions about using substitution to factor before moving to the activity synthesis.

There will be times when a trinomial does not seem to be in the form \(ax^2 + bx + c\).

In some of these cases, we can make a substitution that allows the trinomial to be written in the \(ax^2 + bx + c\) form. This is called factoring by substitution.

It is standard to use \(u\) for the substitution. The substitution for \(u\) might be a variable with an exponent, such as \(x^2\), or it might be a binomial, such as \((x − 3)\).

Factor each trinomial using the substitution method.

  1. \(y^4 − y^2 − 20\)

Answer: \((y^2 + 4)(y^2 − 5)\)

  1. \( (x−5)^2 + 6(x − 5) + 8\)

Answer: \((x − 3)(x − 1)\)

  1. \(x^4 − 3x^2 − 28\)

Answer: \((x^2 − 7)(x^2 + 4)\)

Answers Feedback

That’s correct! Check yourself: Multiply using FOIL to check to make sure the answer is equivalent to the starting polynomial.

\((6x + 2)(x + 1)\)

Incorrect. Let’s try again a different way: These factors do not multiply to the correct \(b\) term. The correct answer is \((x + 2)(6x + 1)\).

\((x − 2)(6x − 1)\)

Incorrect. Let’s try again a different way: These factors do not multiply to the correct \(b\) term. The correct answer is \((x + 2)(6x + 1)\).

Factoring Trinomials Using the “\(ac\)” Method

Another way to factor trinomials of the form \(ax^2 + bx + c\) is the “\(ac\)” method. (The “\(ac\)” method is sometimes called the grouping method.) The “\(ac\)” method is actually an extension of the methods you used in the last section to factor trinomials with a leading coefficient of 1. This method is very structured (that is, step by step), and it always works!

EXAMPLE 1

Factor using the “\(ac\)” method: \(6x^2 + 7x + 2\).

Step 1
Factor any GCF.
Is there a greatest common factor?
\(6x^2 + 7x + 2\)
No!

Step 2
Find the product \(ac\).
\( {\style{color:red}a} {\style{color:red}x} ^ {\style{color:red}2} \; {\style{color:red}+} \; {\style{color:red}b} {\style{color:red}x} \; {\style{color:red}+} \; {\style{color:red}c} \\6x^2+7x+2\) \(6 \cdot 2\)
12

Step 3
Find two numbers, \(m\) and \(n\), that

  • multiply to \(ac\): \(m \cdot n = a \cdot c\).
  • add to \(b\): \(m + n = b\).

Find two numbers that multiply to 12 and add to 7. Both factors must be positive.
\(3 \cdot 4 = 12\)
\(3 + 4 = 7\)

Step 4
Split the middle term using \(m\) and \(n\).

Rewrite \(7x\) as \(3x + 4x\). It would give the same result if we used \(4x + 3x\).

Notice that \(6x^2 + 3x + 4x + 2\) is equal to \(6x^2 + 7x + 2\). We just split the middle term to get a more useful form.

Step 5
Factor by grouping.
\(3x(2x + 1) + 2(2x + 1)\)
\((2x + 1)(3x + 2)\)

Step 6
Check by multiplying the factors.
\((2x + 1)(3x + 2)\)
\(6x^2 + 4x + 3x + 2\)
\(6x^2 + 7x + 2 \checkmark\)

Solution

Here is how to use the “\(ac\)” method to factor a trinomial.

Step 1
Is there a greatest common factor?
\(10y^2-55y+70\)
Yes. The GCF is 5.

Step 2
Factor it.
\(5(2y^2-11y+14)\)

Step 3
The trinomial inside the parentheses has a leading coefficient that is not \(1\).
\(\begin{array}{c}{\style{color:red}a}{\style{color:red}x}^{\style{color:red}2}+{\style{color:red}b}{\style{color:red}x}+{\style{color:red}c}\\5(2y^2-11y+14)\end{array}\)

Find the product \(ac\).
\(ac=28\)

Step 4
Find two numbers that multiply to \(ac\) and add to \(b\).
\((-4)(-7)=28\)
\(-4+(-7)=-11\)

Step 5
Split the middle term.

Step 6
Factor the trinomial by grouping.
\(5(y(2y-7)-2(2y-7))\)
\(5(y-2)(2y-7)\)

Step 7
Check by multiplying all three factors.

Factoring Trinomials Using Substitution

Sometimes a trinomial does not appear to be in the \(ax^2 + bx + c\) form. However, we can often make a thoughtful substitution that will allow us to make it fit the \(ax^2 + bx + c\) form. This is called factoring by substitution. It is standard to use \(u\) for the substitution.

In the form \(ax^2 + bx + c\), the middle term has a variable, \(x\), and its square, \(x^2\), is the variable part of the first term. Look for this relationship as you try to find a substitution.

EXAMPLE 2

Factor by substitution: \(x^4 − 4x^2 − 5\).

The variable part of the middle term is \(x^2\), and its square, \(x^4\), is the variable part of the first term. (We know \((x^2)^2 = x^4)\). If we let \(u = x^2\), we can put our trinomial in the \(ax^2 + bx + c\) form we need to factor it.

Step 1
Rewrite the trinomial to prepare for the substitution.
\(({\style{color:red}x}^{\style{color:red}2})^2-4({\style{color:red}x}^{\style{color:red}2})-5\)

Step 2
Let \(u=x^2\) and substitute.
\({\style{color:red}u}^2-4{\style{color:red}u}-5\)

Step 3
Factor the trinomial.
\((u +1)(u-5)\)

Step 4
Replace \(u\) with \(x^2\).
\(({\style{color:red}x}^{\style{color:red}2}+1)({\style{color:red}x}^{\style{color:red}2}-5)\)

Step 5
Check:

( x 2 + 1 ) ( x 2 − 5 ) x 4 − 5 x 2 + x 2 − 5 x 4 − 4 x 2 − 5 ✓ ( x 2 + 1 ) ( x 2 − 5 ) x 4 − 5 x 2 + x 2 − 5 x 4 − 4 x 2 − 5 ✓

Try It: Factoring Trinomials Using Substitution

Factor by substitution: \((x − 2)^2 + 7(x − 2) + 12\)

In this case, the expression to be substituted is not a monomial. Let \(u = x − 2\).

Write down your answer. Then select the Solution button to compare your work.

Solution

Here is how to factor using substitution.

The binomial in the middle term, \((x − 2)\), is squared in the first term. If we let \(u = x − 2\) and substitute, our trinomial will be in \(ax^2 + bx + c\) form.

Step 1
Rewrite the trinomial to prepare for the substitution.
\({\style{color:red}(}{\style{color:red}x}\;{\style{color:red}-}\;{\style{color:red}2}{\style{color:red})}^2+7{\style{color:red}(}{\style{color:red}x}\;{\style{color:red}-}\;{\style{color:red}2}{\style{color:red})}+12\)

Step 2
Let \(u=x−2\) and substitute.
\(u^2+7u+12\)

Step 3
Factor the trinomial.
\(({\style{color:red}u}+3)({\style{color:red}u}+4)\)

Step 4
Replace \(u\) with \(x−2\).
\(({\style{color:red}(}{\style{color:red}x}\;{\style{color:red}-}\;{\style{color:red}2}{\style{color:red})}+3)({\style{color:red}(}{\style{color:red}x}\;{\style{color:red}-}\;{\style{color:red}2}{\style{color:red})}+4)\)

Step 5
Simplify inside the parentheses.
\((x+1)(x+2)\)

This could also be factored by first multiplying out the \((x − 2)^2\) and the \(7(x − 2)\), combining like terms, and then factoring. Most students prefer the substitution method.
