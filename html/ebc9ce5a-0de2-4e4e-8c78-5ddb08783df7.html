Activity (10 minutes)

In this activity, students practice using algebra to solve systems of linear equations in two variables and checking their solutions. Students do not have to use elimination, but the equations in the first two systems conveniently have opposites for the coefficients of one variable, so one variable can be easily eliminated.

In the last two systems, none of the coefficients of the variables are opposites. Some students may choose to solve the systems by substitution, but the process would be pretty cumbersome. The complication students encounter here motivates the need for another move, which students will explore in the next lesson.

Students who opt to use technology to check their solutions practice choosing tools strategically.

Launch

Keep students in groups of 2 and provide continued access to graphing technology, in case students need it for checking solutions.

If time is limited, ask one partner in each group to solve the first two systems and the other partner to solve the last two, and then ask partners to check each other’s solutions.

Activity Synthesis

Invite students to share their solutions and strategies for the first three systems and how they check their solutions. Then, focus the discussion on the last system. Solicit the strategies students used for approaching that system. If someone solved it by substitution, display the work for all to see. If no one did, ask if it is possible to do. (If time permits, consider asking students to attempt to do so, or demonstrating that strategy to illustrate that it is not very efficient.)

Discuss questions such as:

  • “What happens if we add or subtract the equations?” (No variables are eliminated, so it doesn’t help with solving.)
  • “Why were you able to solve the first three systems by elimination but not the last one? What features were in the equations in those systems but not in the last one?” (In the first three systems, at least one variable in each pair of equations has the same or opposite coefficients, so when the terms are added or subtracted, the result is 0.)
  • We need new moves! Tell students that they will explore another way to solve a system by elimination in an upcoming lesson.

Support for English Language Learners

Before students share their solutions and strategies, present an incorrect method of solving a linear system. For example, “After the first step when solving the system \( 2x+y=9 \) and \( x−y=3 \) by elimination, the resulting equation is \( x=12 \).” Ask students to identify the error, critique the reasoning, and write a correct explanation. As students discuss with a partner, listen for students who clarify the meaning of the solution to a system. Invite students to share their critiques and corrected explanations with the class. Listen for and amplify the language students use to describe what happens when adding two equations together and explain what the solution should be. This helps students evaluate and improve on the written mathematical arguments of others as they clarify how to find the solution of a linear system by using the elimination method.

2.4.3: Additional Resources

The following content is available to students who would like more support based on their experience with the self check. Students will not automatically have access to this content, so you may wish to share it with those who could benefit from it.

Solving a System of Equations by Elimination

Let’s reinforce the steps to solve a system of equations using elimination.


Step 1 - Write both equations in standard form. If any coefficients are fractions, clear them.

Step 2 - Check to see if the coefficients of one variable are opposites. If so, proceed to Step 3. If not, we will cover that in the next lesson.

Step 3 - Add the equations resulting from Step 2 to eliminate one variable.

Step 4 - Solve for the remaining variable.

Step 5 - Substitute the solution from Step 4 into one of the original equations. Then solve for the other variable.

Step 6 - Write the solution as an ordered pair.

Step 7 - Check that the ordered pair is a solution to both original equations.


Example

Let’s look at an example. Solve the following system of equations using elimination:

\( \left\{ \begin{array}{c l} 3x - 3y = -24 \\ -3x + 5y = 30 \end{array}\right. \)

Step 1 - Write both equations in standard form. If any coefficients are fractions, clear them.
\( \left\{ \begin{array}{c l} 3x - 3y = -24 \\ -3x + 5y = 30 \end{array}\right. \)

Step 2 - Check to see if the coefficients of one variable are opposites or equivalent.
The coefficients on the \(x\)-variables are opposites. We can add the equations.

Step 3 - Add the equations to eliminate one variable.
\(\begin{array}{rcl}3x-3y&=&-24\\-3x+5y&=&30\\2y&=&6\end{array}\)

Step 4 - Solve for the remaining variable.
\(\begin{array}{rcl}2y&=&6\\y&=&3\end{array}\)

Step 5 - Substitute the solution from Step 4 into one of the original equations. Then solve for the other variable.
\begin{array}{rcl}3x-3y&=&-24\\3x-3(3)&=&-24\\3x-9&=&-24\\3x&=&-15\\x&=&-5\end{array}

Step 6 - Write the solution as an ordered pair.
\( (-5, 3) \)

Step 7 - Check that the ordered pair is a solution to both original equations.

\(\begin{array}{rcl}3x-3y&=&-24\\3(-5)-3(3)&=&-24\\-15-9&=&-24\\-24&=&-24\checkmark\end{array}\)

\(\begin{array}{rcl}-3x+5y&=&30\\-3(-5)+5(3)&=&30\\15+15&=&30\\30&=&30\checkmark\end{array}\)


Try It: Solving a System of Equations by Elimination

Solve the following system of equations using elimination:

\( \left\{ \begin{array}{c l} 4x - 6y = 8 \\ x + 6y = 17 \end{array}\right. \)

Write down your answer. Then select the solution button to compare your work.

Answer:

Here is how to solve this system of equations using elimination:

Step 1 - Write both equations in standard form. If any coefficients are fractions, clear them.
\(\begin{array}{rcl}4x-6y&=&8\\x+6y&=&17\end{array}\)

Step 2 - Check to see if the coefficients of one variable are opposites or equivalent.
The coefficients on the \(y\)-variables are opposites. We can add the equations.

Step 3 - Add the equations to eliminate one variable.
\(\begin{array}{rcl}4x\;-\;6y\;&=&\;8\\x\;+\;6y\;&=&\;17\\5x\;&=&\;25\end{array}\)

Step 4 - Solve for the remaining variable.
\(\begin{array}{rcl}5x\;&=&\;25\\x\;&=&\;5\end{array}\)

Step 5 - Substitute the solution from Step 4 into one of the original equations. Then solve for the other variable.
\(\begin{array}{rcl}4x\;-\;6y\;&=&\;8\\\;4(5)\;-\;6y\;&=&\;8\\20\;-\;6y\;&=&\;8\\-6y\;&=&\;-12\\y\;&=&\;2\end{array}\)

Step 6 - Write the solution as an ordered pair.
\( (5, 2) \)

Step 7 - Check that the ordered pair is a solution to both original equations.

\(\begin{array}{rcl}4x-6y&=&8\\4(5)-6(2)&=&8\\20-12&=&8\\8&=&8\checkmark\end{array}\)

\(\begin{array}{rcl}x+6y&=&17\\(5)+6(2)&=&17\\5+12&=&17\\17&=&17\checkmark\end{array}\)
