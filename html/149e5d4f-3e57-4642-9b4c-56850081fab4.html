Activity (15 minutes)

In this activity, students use the structure they saw earlier to rewrite squared expressions of the form \((kx+m)^2\), where \(k\) is not 1, into standard form. Through repeated reasoning, students see how the coefficient \(k\) is related to the values of \(a\) and \(b\) when the expression is expanded into \(ax^2+bx+c\), and how the \(m\) is related to \(c\).

As students work, monitor for the following strategies for rewriting the expressions in the first question (from factored form to standard form):

  • Drawing rectangular diagrams.
  • Applying the distributive property, writing out all the expanded terms, and combining like terms.
  • Generalizing that when expanding an expression such as \((4x+1)^2\):
    • The coefficient 4 in \(4x+1\) gets squared, so \(a\) in \(ax^2+bx+c\) is \(4^2\).
    • The coefficient 4 in \(4x+1\) gets multiplied by the constant term 1 twice, so \(b\) is \(2(4)(1)\).
    • The constant term 1 in \(4x+1\) is squared, so \(c\) is \(1^2\).

Identify students using each strategy and invite them to share during the class discussion.

Launch

Explain to students that they will now practice squaring expressions such as \(4x+1\) and \(\frac{1}{2}x+7\), where the coefficient of the linear term is not 1, and writing them in standard form.

Consider arranging students in groups of two and asking them to think quietly about each question before conferring with their partner.

9.4.2: Self Check

After the activity, students will answer the following question to check their understanding of the concepts explored in the activity.

QUESTION:

Find the equivalent factored expression for \(4x^2+12x+9\).

ANSWERS

FEEDBACK

\(4(x^2+3x+9)\)

Incorrect. Let's try again a different way: While 4 is the greatest common factor (GCF) of the first two terms, it is not a factor of 9. The answer is \((2x+3)^2\).

\((2x-3)^2\)

Incorrect. Let's try again a different way: This is equivalent to \(4x^2-12x+9\). The answer is \((2x+3)^2\).

\((2x+3)^2\)

That's correct! Check yourself: When multiplied using distribution, \((2x + 3)(2x + 3)\) equals the given expression.

\((2x+3)(2x-3)\)

Incorrect. Let's try again a different way: This is equivalent to \(4x^2-9\). The answer is \((2x+3)^2\).

Factoring Perfect Square Trinomials

To factor perfect square trinomials, follow the formulas:

\(a^2+2ab+b^2=(a+b)^2\)

\(a^2-2ab+b^2=(a-b)^2\)

Let's look at an example of how to factor perfect square trinomials.

Example

Factor \(9x^2+12x+4\).

Step 1
Does the trinomial fit the perfect square trinomial pattern, \(a^2+2ab+b^2\)? If so, write the first term as \(a^2\) and last constant as \(b^2\).

  • Is the first term a perfect square? Write it as a square, \(a^2\).
    Is \(9x^2\) a perfect square?
    Yes — write it as \((3x)^2\).
    \(\begin{array}{l}9x^2\;+\;12x\;+\;4 \\ (3x)^2\;\end{array}\)
  • Is the last term a perfect square? Write it as a square, \(b^2\).
    Is 4 a perfect square?
    Yes — write it as \((2)^2\).
    \(\begin{array}{l}9x^2\;+\;12x\;+\;4 \\ (3x)^2\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;(2)^2\end{array}\)
  • Check the middle term. Is it \(2ab\)?
    Is \(12x\) twice the product of \(3x\) and 2?
    Does it match?
    Yes, so we have a perfect trinomial.

\(\begin{array}{c}9x^2\;\;\;\;\;\;+\;\;\;\;\;\;12x\;\;\;\;\;\;+\;\;\;\;\;\;4\; \\ (3x)^2\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;(2)^2 \\ (3x)^2\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;(2)^2 \\ {\style{color:green}\searrow}\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;{\style{color:green}\swarrow} \\ 2(3x)(2) \\ 12x\;\end{array}\)

Step 2
Factor the binomial into: \(a^2+2ab+b^2 = (a+b)^2 \)
\(9x^2+12x+4\)
\(\begin{array}{l}a^2\;\;\;\;\;\;+2\cdot a\cdot b+b^2 \\ \;({\style{color:red}3}{\style{color:red}x})^2\;+2 \cdot {\style{color:red}3}{\style{color:red}x} \cdot {\style{color:purple}2}+{\style{color:purple}2}^2\; \\ \;\;\;\;\;\;\;(a+b)^2\; \\ \;\;\;\;\;({\style{color:red}3}{\style{color:red}x}+{\style{color:green}2})^{\style{color:green}2}\end{array}\)

Step 3
Check.
\((3x+2)^2\)
\((3x)^2+2 \cdot 3x \cdot 2+2^2\)

Try It: Factoring Perfect Square Trinomials

Factor \(9x^2-30x+25\).

Write down your answer, then select the solution button to compare your work.

Solution

Here is how to factor a perfect square trinomial:

Step 1
Write the first term as \(a^2\) and the last constant as \(b^2\).
\(a^2=(3x)^2\), \(b^2=5^2\)

Make sure the middle term is \(2ab\).
\(2ab=2(3x)(5)=30x\)

Step 2
Factor the binomial into: \(a^2-2ab+b^2=(a-b)^2\).
\((3x-5)^2\)

Step 3
Check.

\((3x-5)(3x-5)\)
\(=9x^2-15x-15x+25\)
\(=9x^2-30x+25\)
