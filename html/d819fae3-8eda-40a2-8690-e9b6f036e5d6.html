Activity (20 minutes)

This activity allows students to write radicals as terms with rational exponents and vice versa.

Launch

Arrange students into groups of 2.


Student Activity

If \(\sqrt[n]{a^m}\) is a real number and \(n\;\geq\;2\), then \(\sqrt[n]{a^m}\;=\;a^\frac mn\).

So radicals can be rewritten as rational exponents.

  1. With a partner, write each radical as a term with rational exponents on your own paper. Then, turn and talk to your partner. Check your answers when you are ready.
    1. \(\sqrt[8]x\)

      Answer: \(x^\frac18\)

    2. \(\sqrt[3]{x^2}\)

      Answer: \(x^\frac23\)

    3. \(\sqrt[11]{x^5}\)

      Answer: \(x^\frac5{11}\)

  2. Now, with a partner, rewrite each term as an equivalent radical. Check your answer to each.
    1. \(x^\frac12\)

      Answer: \(\sqrt[2]x\;\) or \(\;\sqrt x\)

    2. \(x^\frac17\)

      Answer: \(\sqrt[7]x\;\)

    3. \(x^\frac45\)

      Answer: \(\sqrt[5]{x^4}\;\)

Anticipated Misconceptions

When writing radicals as terms with rational exponents, it is important that the rational exponent has the original power on the top of the fraction and the index/root on the bottom.

A graphic organizer can help students as a reference for this.

Activity Synthesis

Invite pairs to share their answers. Allow students to make their own examples and then trade with other partner groups if time allows.

  • First, have each pair write an example as a radical and switch with another pair. Then each pair writes the term with the rational exponent that matches their radical.
  • Have each pair write a term with a rational exponent and then switch with another pair. Each pair writes the term as a radical.

5.2.3: Self Check

After the activity, students will answer the following question to check their understanding of the concepts explored in the activity.


QUESTION:

Rewrite \(\sqrt[10]{x^7}\) as a term with a rational exponent.

ANSWERS

FEEDBACK

\(x^\frac{10}7\)

Incorrect. Let’s try again a different way: The 7 is the exponent, and the 10 is the index. The answer is \(x^\frac{7}{10}\).

\(x^\frac{7}{10}\)

That’s correct! Check yourself: The exponent gets written over the root.

\(10x^7\)

Incorrect. Let’s try again a different way: The 10 is the root, not a coefficient. The answer is \(x^\frac{7}{10}\).

\(\frac7{10}x\)

Incorrect. Let’s try again a different way: The fraction should be the exponent. The answer is \(x^\frac{7}{10}\).

Rewriting Radicals

When writing radicals as a term with a rational exponent, the exponent becomes the numerator of the fraction in the exponent, the root or index.

In the example above, the 2 is the exponent and becomes the top of the fraction. The 5 is the index and becomes the bottom of the fraction.

Try It: Rewriting Radicals

Write \(\sqrt[6]{x^5}\) as a term with a rational exponent.

Write down your answer, then select the Solution button to compare your work.

Solution

Here is how to rewrite the radical expression:


Step 1
Identify the index.
6

Step 2
Identify the exponent (inside the radical).
5

Step 3
Write the power over the root.
\(x^\frac56\)
