Warm Up (5 minutes)

This warm up encourages students to quantify the number of squares in a geometric pattern by looking for and making use of structure. The thinking involved here prepares students to reason about other patterns that illustrate quadratic relationships.

Launch

Arrange students in groups of two. Give students 2–3 minutes of quiet think time, and then ask them to share their thinking with their partner.

Student Activity

How does each expression represent the number of small squares in the figure?

  1. Expression A: \(6 \cdot 8−2 \cdot 3\)

    Answer: There are 6 rows with 8 small squares, but from this, 2 rows of 3 small squares have been removed.
  1. Expression B: \(4 \cdot 8+2 \cdot 5\)

    Answer: There are 4 rows of 8 small squares, and to this, 2 rows of 5 small squares are added.
  1. Expression C: \(8+8+8+8+5+5\)

    Answer: This counts each row, starting from the bottom.
  1. Expression D: \(5 \cdot 6+3 \cdot 4\)

    Answer: There are 5 columns of 6 small squares and 3 columns of 4 small squares.

Activity Synthesis

Make sure students see how each expression tells us about a way the squares can be counted or how the area of the figure can be determined. If time permits, ask students if they could generate a different expression that also represents the number of squares in the figure.
