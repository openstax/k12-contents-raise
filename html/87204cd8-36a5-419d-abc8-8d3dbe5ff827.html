Activity (15 minutes)

Noah is having trouble solving two equations. In each case, he took steps that he thought were acceptable but ended up with statements that are clearly not true.

Analyze Noah’s work on each equation and the moves he made. Were they acceptable moves? Why do you think he ended up with a false equation?

Discuss your observations with your group and be prepared to share your conclusions. If you get stuck, consider solving each equation.

Example 1 Example 2

Step 1 - Original equation.

\( x+6 =4x+1-3x \)

Step 2 - Apply the Commutative Property.

\( x+6 =4x-3x+1 \)

Step 3 - Combine like terms.        

\( x+6 =x+1 \)

Step 4 - Subtract \(x\) from each side.

\( 6 =1 \)

 

 

Step 1 - Original equation.

\( 2(5+x)-1=3x+9 \)

Step 2 - Apply the Distributive Property.

\( 10+2x-1 =3x+9 \)

Step 3 - Subtract 10 from each side.

 \( 2x-1 =3x-1 \)

Step 4 - Add 1 to each side.       

\( 2x=3x \)

Step 5 - Divide each side by \(x\).

\( 2= 3 \)

Why did Noah have no solution for either equation?

Answer:

Your answer may vary, but here is a sample.


Example 1: All the moves that Noah made were acceptable, but because he ended up with a false statement, that means there is no value of \( x \) that makes the first equation true. We can see it in the third line: \( x + 6 = x + 1 \). There is no number that can make the equation true.


Example 2: Noah's moves seem acceptable except for the last one. If he had subtracted \( 2x \) from each side of the equation, he would have \( 0 = x \). Instead, he divided both sides by \( x \) which would give an undefined number if \( x\;\;is\;\;0 \). 

Noah tried to solve a third equation and ended up with a true statement, without variables.

Step 1 - Original equation.

\( 2 (4 + x) - 2 = 2x + 6 \)

Step 2 - Apply the Distributive Property.

\( 8+ 2x - 2 =2x + 6 \)

Step 3 - Combine Like Terms.

\( 2x +6 = 2x +6 \)

Step 4 - Subtract 6 from both sides.       

\( 2x = 2x \)

Step 5 - Subtract \(2x\) from both sides.

\( 0=0 \)


Since there is not a variable and the statement is true, any value of \(x\) would be a solution for the original equation. So, this equation has infinitely many solutions.

Anticipated Misconceptions

Some students may point to a step that is valid and mistakenly identify it as an error. For instance, in the first set of steps, they may object to replacing \( +1 - 3x \) with \( -3x+1 \), thinking that it should be rearranged to \( +3x-1 \). Push their reasoning with a simpler example. Ask, for instance, if \( +7 - 4 \) is equivalent to \( +4 - 7 \). Remind students that we can think of \( +7 -4 \) as \( +7 + (-4) \) and then apply the commutative property of addition to get \( -4 + 7 \).

If students hypothesize about two equations being equivalent but are not sure how to check if it’s actually the case, suggest that a good way to check is by finding the solution to one equation, then checking whether that value is also a solution to the second equation.

Activity Synthesis

Invite students to share what they thought was the problem with Noah’s work. They are likely to say that Noah seems to have performed allowable moves and did them correctly. Then, draw students’ attention to the second-to-last step: \( 2x = 3x \). Ask students:

  • “Earlier, when looking at \( x + 6 = x + 1 \), we reasoned that there’s no value of \( x \) that could make this equation true. Is there a value of \( x \) that could make \( 2x = 3x \) true?” (Yes, 0.)
  • “If there is a value that can make \( 2x=3x \) true, what do we know about the original equation? Is it also true?” (Yes, they are all equivalent equations.) “What is its solution?” (0)
  • “So the equations are true up through the third step. The last step is where we have a false statement, after Noah shows division by \( x \). If \( x \) is 0, what might be the problem with dividing by \( x \)?” (If \( x \) is 0, then we are dividing both sides by 0, which gives an undefined result.)

Explain that dividing by the variable in the equation is not done because if the solution happens to be 0, it could lead us to thinking that there is no solution while in fact there is (the solution is the number 0).

Revisit the lists of acceptable and unacceptable moves compiled in earlier activities. Add “dividing by the variable” and “dividing by 0” to the list of unacceptable moves.

1.7.3: Self Check

After the activity, students will answer the following question to check their understanding of the concepts explored in the activity.

QUESTION:

Which of the following equations has a solution?

ANSWERS FEEDBACK
\( 3x = x + 18 \) That’s correct! When \(x\) is subtracted from each side and each side is divided by 2, \( x \) will have a solution, \( x = 9 \).
\( 2x = 2x + 1 \) Incorrect. Let’s try this another way: There are no values for \(x\) that will make this equation true. The correct answer is \( 3x = x + 18 \). 
\( x + 3 = x -2 \) Incorrect. Let’s try this another way: Subtracting \(x\) from both sides results in a false equation, \( 3 = -2 \). There are no values for \(x\) that will make this equation true. The correct answer is \( 3x = x + 18 \). 
\( 10x + 10 = 7x + 3x \) Incorrect. Let’s try this another way: Combining the like terms on the right side and subtracting \(10x\) from both sides results in a false statement, \( 10 = 0 \). There are no values for \(x\) that will make this equation true. The correct answer is \( 3x = x + 18 \).

Equations Without Solutions 

What happens when we solve \( 5z = 5z - 1\)?

Subtract \(5z\) to get the \(z\) terms together on one side.

\( 5z-5z = 5z-5z - 1 \)

Simplify—the \(z\) terms are gone!

\( 0 = 1 \)

Since 0 does not equal \(-1\), this equation led to a false statement. There are no solutions to this equation.

Example:

Solve \( 5m+3(9+3m)=2(7m−11) \). 

Original equation.

\(5m+3(9+3m)=2(7m−11)\)

Distribute.

\(5m +27+9m =14m-22\)

Combine like terms.

\(14m +27 =14m -22\)

Subtract \(14m\) from both sides so the \(m\) terms are together.

\(14m -14m +27 =14m -14m-22\)

Simplify. (The \(m\) terms are gone!)

\(27=-22\)

The equation \(27 = -22\) is NOT a true statement. The equation \(5m+3(9+3m)=2(7m−11)\) has no solutions.

Try It: Equations Without Solutions

Solve \( 3x - 4 = 3(x -2) \) and determine the number of solutions.

Write down your answer, and then select the solution button to compare your work.

Solution

Here is how to solve this linear equation and determine the number of solutions:

Original equation. \( 3x - 4 = 3(x -2) \)
Distribute.  \( 3x - 4 = 3x -6 \)
Subtract \( 3x \) from each side so the \( x \)’s are together.  \( 3x-3x - 4 = 3x -3x-6 \)
Simplify. (The \(x\)’s are gone!)  \( -4 = -6 \)

The final step, \(-4 = -6\) is NOT a true statement so the equation \( 3x - 4 = 3(x -2) \) has no solution.

Infinitely Many Solutions 

What if an equation results in a true statement without variables?

The equations \(-4 = -4\), \(5 =5\), and \(0=0\) are all true statements because one side is equal to the other. This means that any value of the variable in the original equation would be a solution. When this happens in the final step of an equation, there are infinitely many solutions.

Example: Solve \( 2y +6 =2(y+3) \)

Distribute.

\( 2y+6 = 2y +6 \)

Subtract \( 2y \) to bring the \(y\)’s to one side.

\( 2y-2y+6 = 2y-2y +6 \)
Simplify—the \(y\)’s are gone!  \( 6 = 6 \)

We say the solution to the equation is all of the real numbers. An equation like this that is true for any value of the variable is called an identity.

The solution of an identity is all real numbers.

Try It: Infinitely Many Solutions

Solve \(10x - 4 = 2(5x -2)\) and determine the number of solutions.

Write down your answer, and then select the Solution button to compare your work.

Solution

Here is how to solve this linear equation using a general strategy:

Original equation.  \( 10x - 4 = 2(5x -2) \)
Distribute.  \( 10x - 4 =10x - 4 \)
Subtract \( 10x \) from each side so the \( x \)’s are together.  \( 10x-10x - 4 =10x - 10x - 4 \)
Simplify—the \(x\)’s are gone!  \( -4 = -4 \)

The final step, \(-4 = -4\), is a true statement, and the original equation, \( 10x - 4 = 2(5x - 2) \), has infinitely many solutions.
